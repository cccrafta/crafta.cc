# The Crafta Editorial Framework

Every piece of Crafta writing moves through three lenses. They don't need to be explicit sections — they can be woven together — but all three must be present in the thinking behind every post.

## 1. Observation (Truth)

See the subject as it actually is. No opinion yet — rigorous, honest documentation.

- **History** — origin, context, why it existed
- **Materiality** — what it's made of, how the material behaves, its properties
- **Tectonics** — how it's constructed, the structural decisions, joints, seams
- **Comfort** — how it feels in use, how it performs against the body or in practice
- **Craft context** — what makers and artisans know about it that consumers don't
- **Reference value** — what principles does it embody, what can be learned from studying it

**God is in the details — and details are observed, not stated.**

There are two levels of detail:
- **Facts** (necessary but not sufficient): the weight in ounces, the year, the town name
- **Observations** (where the writing comes alive): what it feels like under your thumb, how the fabric catches light, the way a seam sits when the garment is worn vs laid flat, the sound a button makes against a placket

The writer must *look* at the subject — really look — the way a craftsman examines a piece of material before cutting it. Describe what you see, feel, and notice. Not what you know from a spec sheet, but what you'd notice if you held the garment in your hands, turned it inside out, rubbed the fabric between your fingers, held it up to light.

This is the difference between "the waffle knit creates air pockets for insulation" (a fact) and "run your thumb across a waffle thermal and the grid catches against skin — each cell a tiny cup of still air that the body warms and the fabric holds" (an observation).

Facts tell the reader the thing exists. Observations make the reader feel like they're holding it.

**Balance is essential.** A passage that is all facts reads like a reference entry — accurate but lifeless. A passage that is all observation reads like impressionism — evocative but ungrounded. The best writing moves between them: a fact establishes what something is, then an observation shows what it's like to encounter it. They serve different purposes and need each other. Let the subject determine the ratio — a paragraph about construction history needs more facts; a paragraph about how a fabric behaves on the body needs more observation.

The goal: understand the thing before forming any opinion about it.

## 2. Transformation (Relevance)

Look at the same subject through the lens of time, market, and people.

- **Then vs now** — how has it changed, what was gained, what was lost
- **Current relevance** — does it still matter, to whom, and why
- **Value** — is it undervalued, overvalued, or misunderstood
- **Potential** — what could it become, where is it heading
- **Multiple perspectives** — how do designers, craftspeople, consumers, and subcultures each see it differently

The goal: understand where the thing sits in the world right now.

## 3. Stance (Beauty as Recognition)

Beauty is not an aesthetic judgment. It is a recognition — discovered through honest observation and understanding, not declared.

When something serves a noble or good purpose, when it is respected and valued for genuine reasons, when its form aligns with its function and its necessity — that is where beauty lives.

The Stance is the craftsman's eye:
- "This is well-made, and it matters, and here's why it will continue to matter"
- It's the point of view of someone who wants to be a craftsman, designer, tailor, artist
- It asks: is this of value to us and to the future of innovation, design, and art?
- It recognizes when purpose and implication are in harmony

The Stance is not always positive. Sometimes honest observation reveals that something has lost its purpose, was made for the wrong reasons, or no longer serves the people who use it. That too is a valid stance — recognizing the absence of beauty is as important as recognizing its presence.

**Beauty = when the evidence reveals that something has integrity.**

## 4. Narrative (Coherence)

Observation, Transformation, and Stance are lenses — they tell you *how* to see. Narrative tells you *why* the reader should follow along. Without it, even the most detailed writing is a catalog of unconnected insights.

**Every article answers a question.** The title poses it — implicitly or explicitly. Each paragraph advances the answer. The reader should feel, at every point, that the writing is going somewhere, and that each detail serves the journey.

### How narrative works in practice:
- **The title is a promise.** "How What You Wear Under Your Jacket Changes Everything" promises that the reader will understand *why* the invisible layer matters and *how* it changes the outcome. Every paragraph must deliver on that promise.
- **Each paragraph earns the next.** Paragraph 1 sets up a question or tension. Paragraph 2 doesn't just add a new topic — it builds on what paragraph 1 established. The connection can be explicit ("this same principle applies to...") or implicit (the reader feels the logic), but it must be there.
- **Details serve the narrative, not the reverse.** A detail about waffle knit cells trapping air is only valuable if it helps answer the article's question. Ask: does this detail advance the argument? If yes, include it. If it's interesting but irrelevant, save it for another post.
- **The reader's question evolves.** Start with something practical ("what should I wear under my jacket?"), deepen it ("why does the choice matter more than we think?"), and resolve it with the stance ("because the invisible layer is where honesty lives").

### The test:
After writing, remove the title and read the paragraphs in sequence. Can you feel the logic building? Does each paragraph depend on the one before it? Could you summarize the article's argument in one sentence? If not, the narrative isn't there yet.

## How to Apply

When writing about any topic:

1. **Start with the question** — what does the title promise? What will the reader understand by the end that they didn't at the beginning?
2. **Observe** — gather facts, history, material reality, construction details. See it clearly. But observe *in service of the question* — not everything you know, only what advances the narrative.
3. **Transform** — place it in context. What does it mean now? How has it changed? Connect the observations into a developing argument.
4. **Take a stance** — the honest recognition that emerges. This is the answer to the question the title posed.

The stance emerges from the observation. It is not imposed onto it. And the narrative is the thread that connects observation to stance — without it, the reader has details but no understanding.

## Quality Essentials — Non-Negotiable

These must be present in every piece of Crafta writing. They are not optional.

### Research Integrity
- Every factual claim must be traceable to a source in `bank/Sources/`
- Cross-reference across multiple publications — single-source claims must be flagged
- Research before writing. Always. The depth of understanding behind a post is what separates it from generic content

### The Three Lenses
- Observation, Transformation, and Stance must all be present — woven together, not as rigid sections
- Observation without Transformation is a spec sheet
- Transformation without Observation is opinion without foundation
- Stance without both is posturing

### Voice
- The craftsman's eye — someone who respects the subject enough to look closely
- Authoritative but curious, never arrogant
- Specific over general, always
- No superlatives, no first person, no marketing language
- Let the evidence speak — show why something has integrity, don't tell the reader it does

### Structure
- Original prose only — no bullet points or lists within the post
- Open with a concrete observation, not a definition
- Close with the stance — the recognition that emerged
- Content in `<p>` tags for WordPress publication
- 300-500 words standard, 500-700 deep dive

### Source Tracking
- Every post has a `## Sources Referenced` section in its vault note
- Sources link to `bank/Sources/References/` notes
- The source trail must be complete enough that any claim can be verified
