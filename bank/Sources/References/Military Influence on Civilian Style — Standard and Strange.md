## Summary
Detailed cultural history of how military surplus entered American civilian wardrobes 1945-1972, covering the garment shortage context, specific surplus stores named, counterculture adoption, and the symbolic meaning of surplus as anti-establishment dress by 1968.

## Key Claims
- Post-WWII garment shortage: New York Times reported in 1945 that the garment industry could only produce half the normal 24 million annual suits — veterans wore military clothing by necessity, not only choice
- Early surplus garments adopted: M-1943 Field Jackets, B-15 cloth flight jackets, leather A-2 flight jackets, wool overcoats, peacoats
- Students dyed uniforms in blue, brown, black, or bottle green to remove military appearance; swapped brass buttons
- Named surplus stores: Sam's Army & Navy Store (Connecticut); Army-Navy Surplus on 42nd Street (New York City); local store in North Hampton, Massachusetts
- By 1950s-60s, army-navy surplus stores were "practically everywhere" in cities and towns
- Military ponchos became trendy at Smith College by 1964; fatigue shirts cost $2 per matching set at University of Texas surplus store
- By 1968, GQ article stated "only hippies wear Army-Navy surplus" — surplus had become counterculture signifier
- Black Panther Party, SDS, anti-war protesters all wore surplus — surplus as symbol of rebellion against state authority
- By mid-1970s, New York Times ran headline "Surplus Chic" — mainstream acceptance
- No specific CPO shirt mentions in this article — but establishes the cultural pipeline that carried CPOs from navy to civilian
