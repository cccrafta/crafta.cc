## Summary
Cultural history of the Harrington jacket focusing on its Hollywood connections and subcultural adoption. Emphasizes the Fraser tartan story, Lord Lovat's WWII heroism, and the jacket's evolution from sportswear to subcultural icon.

## Key Claims
- **Baracuta G9 created 1937** by brothers John and Isaac Miller at their Manchester factory, designed for golfers
- **Fraser tartan lining added 1938** — Lord Lovat (Simon Fraser, 24th Chieftain) granted rights to use Fraser family tartan
- **Lord Lovat's WWII service** added prestige to the tartan — he was a decorated WWII commander
- **Grenfell alternative origin**: Walter Haythornthwaite met Sir Wilfred Grenfell in 1922, inspired by concept that "clothing must be windproof, but must breathe"
- **James Dean wore Harrington** in *Rebel Without a Cause* (1955) giving it "a rascally charm"
- **Elvis Presley** wore it in *King Creole* (1958)
- **Ryan O'Neal popularized "Harrington" name** via character Rodney Harrington in 1960s TV series *Peyton Place*
- **Steve McQueen** wore navy cotton version in *The Thomas Crown Affair* (1968), embodying "the true essence of stylistic masculinity"
- **Gregory Peck** wore beige version at 1963 Venice Film Festival
- **Mid-1960s subcultural adoption**: Post-Mod skinheads, Punks, and British rockers embraced the jacket
