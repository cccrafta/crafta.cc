## Summary
Comprehensive overview of cashmere wool covering fiber biology, geographic production, harvesting, processing, grading standards, and historical use from Kashmir to global trade.

## Key Claims
- Cashmere manufactured in Mongolia, Nepal, and Kashmir for thousands of years; references to woolen shawls appear in texts from 3rd century BCE to 11th century CE
- Capra hircus laniger (Pashmina goat) produces the fiber; average diameter 14–19 microns
- FTC Wool Products Labeling Act: average diameter must not exceed 19µm; no more than 3% of fibers exceed 30µm
- China largest producer: 19,200 metric tons (in hair) per year as of 2016; Mongolia second: 8,900 tons
- Changthangi goats of Himalayan highlands produce finest fibers: 12–13 microns
- Annual yield per goat: as low as 150 grams; a sweater requires 3–6 goats; an overcoat 30–40
- Global annual pure cashmere production: ~6,500 metric tons vs. 2 million for sheep's wool
- De-hairing separates fine undercoat from guard hair; processing reduces yield by ~50%
- High-quality sweaters contain less than 1% coarse hair; coating fabrics less than 5%
