## Summary
Documentation of the Harrington jacket's adoption across British youth subcultures from the 1960s through punk era. Covers Mods, Skinheads, and Punks, with context on how each group wore and interpreted the same garment differently.

## Key Claims
- **Mods adopted early 1960s** — worn clean and sharp, perfect over slim suit or polo shirt, ideal for Vespa riding
- **Skinhead adoption mid-to-late 1960s** — part of standard wardrobe alongside bomber jackets, denim jackets, Dr. Martens boots
- **Original skinhead culture** (before political associations) represented working-class pride and unpretentious, durable fashion
- **Skinhead color preferences**: black, red, and fawn/tan most popular, always with tartan lining as standard
- **Complementary brands**: worn with Ben Sherman button-down shirts, Fred Perry polo shirts, Brutus shirts, Warrior, Jaytex
- **Early skinhead origins** involved unity of blacks and whites, emerged from Mod movement as they started listening to Jamaican reggae
- **Punk adoption 1970s** — academic paper titled "Put on Your Boots and Harrington!: The ordinariness of 1970s UK punk dress" documents continuity from skinhead to punk dress
- **The Clash wore customized versions** in rebellious style
- **Subcultural continuity**: Dr. Marten boots and Harrington jacket were worn by skinheads who preceded punk, showing material continuity between movements
- **Punk/ska pairing**: often worn with Dr. Martens boots and distressed jeans
- **Put This On observation**: "once a working man's jacket" that helped it become mod icon, originally sold for under $100
- **Japanese subculture adoption**: called "Swing jacket" or "Swing Top"
