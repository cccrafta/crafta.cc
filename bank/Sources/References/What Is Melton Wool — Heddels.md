## Summary
Technical primer on melton wool — covers the fulling/milling process, surface properties, weather resistance, and garment applications. Describes how it's made and why it performs the way it does.

## Key Claims
- Melton is woven in twill form, then fulled (milled): subjected to moisture, heat, and friction to increase thickness and compactness
- Process stages: scouring (removes oil and dirt) → milling (thickening) → shaving (uniform surface finish)
- The quasi-felted texture enables: minimal fraying, water-wicking properties, fair wind resistance
- Described as "arguably the warmest woolen" — the dense fulled structure traps significant air
- Whitesville uses 30 oz. melton wool for their award jackets
- Deployed on CPO shirts, peacoats, and robust outerwear garments
- Because of felted structure, melton does not fray when cut — can be left with raw edges
- No specific aging or patina data provided in article
