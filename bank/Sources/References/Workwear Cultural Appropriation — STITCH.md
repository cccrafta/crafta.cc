## Summary
Analysis of the class dynamics in the luxury workwear trend, examining how blue-collar uniforms are appropriated by fashion. Raises the uncomfortable irony of rich people in New York and LA obsessing over clothes created for and still worn by manual laborers.

## Key Claims
- "Rich people in New York and Los Angeles are now obsessed with clothes created for and still worn by blue-collar workers"
- Chanel's iconic little black dress was directly inspired by maid uniforms — class appropriation in fashion is not new
- Retailers "deliberately distressing the material to make jeans look damaged as a product of hard labor"
- Workers manufacturing distressed jeans use salt sprays that cause blindness and chemical bleaches causing severe burns
- Workwear is "an anti-fashion uniform for many who do [wear it]; people who wear it out of necessity, rather than as an accessory"
- Workwear functions as "a cipher of social standing, distinguishing the hierarchy between blue-collar and white-collar workers"
- On TikTok, people "thank" blue-collar workers for donating used workwear to thrift for fashion
- The workwear trend grows precisely as the industrial sector fades
