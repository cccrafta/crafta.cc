## Summary
Heddels deep dive on the CPO as the quintessential shirt jacket — covers military origins, construction details including original 1939 single-pocket model and late-1940s dual-pocket evolution, post-WWII civilian adoption, mass retail phase, and contemporary maker landscape.

## Key Claims
- Officially titled "Shirt, Wool, Blue" — introduced to U.S. Navy in the 1930s, assigned to Chief Petty Officers
- Cut from navy wool flannel, roomy fit for layering over chunky knits and thermals as outerwear
- Issued in scores throughout WWII; minimal silhouette changes
- Second chest pocket added in late 1940s — dual-pocket version used until phaseout
- Late 1960s: Navy phases out 100% wool CPO
- Post-military: discharged naval officers wore CPOs as civilians; available in Army/Navy surplus stores
- 1960s-70s: sold by Sears, Roebuck, J.C. Penney, and Montgomery Ward in multiple colors and patterns (plaids, jacquards)
- Richard Widmark appeared in a CPO shirt in Down to the Sea in Ships (1949)
- Original 1939 "1st Model" had single left-side pocket with scalloped flap; second pocket added in the 1940s
- Navy-issued versions had anchor-etched plastic buttons
- Buttons positioned on placket, cuffs, and pockets
