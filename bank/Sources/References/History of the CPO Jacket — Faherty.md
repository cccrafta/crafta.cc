## Summary
Faherty's history of the CPO jacket tracing U.S. Navy origins through post-WWII working-class civilian adoption and 1960s-70s outdoors popularity, culminating in the brand's own contemporary cotton/canvas interpretations.

## Key Claims
- U.S. Navy introduced CPO shirt in 1930s as standard uniform for Chief Petty Officers; uses heavyweight wool for heat retention in wet conditions
- Post-WWII: veterans brought CPOs home; became popular among fishermen, dock workers, and outdoor laborers in cold-weather regions like Northeastern U.S.
- 1960s-70s: gained widespread popularity among outdoor enthusiasts — hikers, hunters, campers; military surplus stores depleted by demand
- Pendleton, L.L. Bean, Eddie Bauer among the brands that adopted the CPO silhouette into civilian outerwear
- Modern Faherty versions use canvas and loopback knit rather than wool — a significant material departure from the original
