## Summary
In-depth interview with Stephanie Collie (Season 1 costume designer) covering fabric choices, construction philosophy, the Leone Western influence on the overcoats, and the specific decisions that made the show's costumes historically grounded but visually heightened.

## Key Claims
- Tweed was the primary material — sourced from Scottish factories; "adds something extra" to the suits
- Moleskin and velvet also incorporated
- Period-appropriate buttons and cufflinks: sourced from vintage fairs rather than contemporary reproductions
- Shorter pants and fitted cuts: informed by post-WWI material rationing and material scarcity
- Long overcoats: inspired by Sergio Leone's Western aesthetic — "tailored lighter so they moved dramatically"
- Costumes adapted to each performer's physique, not standardized sizing
- Garments "real, believable" and historically aligned with 1919 Birmingham — with "small adjustments" for contemporary audiences
- Avoided modern fabrics that would appear anachronistic on screen
- Lead actor (Cillian Murphy): custom-made; supporting cast: rented pieces subsequently tailored
