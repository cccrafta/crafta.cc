## Summary
Primary historical account of the Kashmir shawl's arrival in European fashion via Napoleon's Egyptian campaign, Empress Joséphine's collection, and the subsequent Parisian craze that established cashmere as a luxury textile in the West.

## Key Claims
- Kashmir shawls first appeared in French fashion magazines and portraits in 1790
- Arrived in significant quantities after Napoleon's Egyptian campaign (1798–1801)
- General Napoleon brought shawls back from Egypt; created an immediate sensation in Paris
- Empress Joséphine initially wrote to her son Eugène: "I find them hideous... I doubt very much if they will ever become fashionable"
- Joséphine eventually collected 300–400 Kashmir shawls, including 150 at her home in Navarre "of incomparable beauty and great value"
- Post-Revolutionary fashion (empire-waist muslin dresses, exposed shoulders) made shawls functionally and aesthetically necessary
- By 1780s, imitation textile centers in Norwich, Edinburgh, and Lyon
- William-Louis Ternaux began production at Reims in 1799 using Spanish merino; by 1811 experimenting with real Tibetan cashmere
- Treaty of Amritsar (1846) required Maharaja of Kashmir to send three pairs of Kashmiri shawls annually to Queen Victoria
