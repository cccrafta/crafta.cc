## This Week in Craft & Fashion

### The Big Story — White Oak Looms Are Coming Back

The most significant textile story this week: Mount Vernon Mills has acquired all 45 historic Draper X3 shuttle looms from the shuttered Cone Mills White Oak plant in Greensboro, North Carolina — the looms that produced American selvedge denim until White Oak closed in 2017. They've partnered with KaKa Cotton to relocate the looms to Trion, Georgia. Heddels ran a full podcast episode with Mount Vernon president Bill Rogers: first rolls of American selvedge expected in weeks, not months. The same deal also brings 45 Picanol President shuttle looms that could revive Cramerton Army Cloth. This is the most concrete development in American selvedge manufacturing since White Oak's closure.

### Denim

- **Southeast Asian raw denim** (Denimhunters) — Feature on 9 brands from Indonesia, Thailand, Malaysia, Vietnam. Indonesian standouts: Oldblue Co. - **GUSTIN Featherweight Broken Twill** — Affordable lightweight denim alternative to heavy raw selvedge.

- **Freenote Cloth Natural Indigo Modesto** — Over a year of development for naturally indigo-dyed selvedge. Slow production as intentional craft.

## Worth Watching

**American selvedge revival**: The White Oak/Mount Vernon Mills story is the most consequential thing happening in denim manufacturing right now. If the looms ship selvedge within weeks as projected, it fundamentally changes the "only Japan makes selvedge" narrative that's defined the market since 2017. Worth tracking closely — especially whether Mount Vernon pursues Cramerton Army Cloth revival, which would be significant for military workwear heritage.

**Geography of heritage craft**: Denimhunters' Southeast Asian feature points to something real — Indonesian and Thai brands have been building genuine craft credentials for over a decade, largely in the shadow of Japanese coverage. Natural dyestuffs from mangosteen and persimmon in particular are worth more attention.
