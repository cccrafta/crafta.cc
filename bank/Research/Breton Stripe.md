## Overview
The mariniere is a tubular-knit cotton shirt with horizontal blue and white stripes, codified as French naval uniform in 1858. Named for the sailors of Brittany (Bretagne), it migrated from military workwear to civilian fashion through geographic proximity rather than marketing, and was adopted by a succession of cultural icons — each independently, each for practical rather than aesthetic reasons.

## Historical Timeline
- **1858** — French parliament passes the Act of France, codifying the mariniere as naval uniform. Admiral Hamelin specifies: 21 white stripes, each twice the width of the blue stripes
- **1889** — Saint James begins knitting in Normandy
- **1913** — Coco Chanel opens Deauville boutique, adopts the mariniere for her collections
- **1920s** — Breton becomes shorthand for French style through Chanel
- **1938** — Armor Lux begins operating from Quimper
- **1940s** — French beatniks adopt it
- **1943** — Picasso paints self-portrait in stripes during Nazi occupation
- **1944** — Picasso tells journalist he always wears a sailor's shirt — opens outer shirt to prove it
- **1950s** — James Dean wears it in New York
- **1960** — Jean Seberg wears it in Breathless
- **1960s** — Andy Warhol at the Factory, Brigitte Bardot in Saint-Tropez
- **1978** — Jean Paul Gaultier wears mariniere to his first men's show
- **1983** — Gaultier makes the stripe his house signature with Toy Boy collection
- **Present** — Saint James and Armor Lux still produce on original principles

## Why "Breton"
Named after Brittany (Bretagne), the northwestern French peninsula where the Navy was historically based. Most sailors came from there. Also called:
- *Mariniere* — "the sailor"
- *Tricot raye* — "striped knit"
- *Chandail* — from "marchand d'ail" (garlic merchant) — onion sellers from Brittany wore them when selling across the Channel

## Construction Details
- **Knit:** Tubular — no side seams (nothing to snag on rigging, easy to launder)
- **Cotton:** Dense, heavy enough to hold wind and shed spray
- **Stripes:** 21 white stripes (twice the width of blue) — visibility at sea
- **Neckline:** Wide boat neck opening at the collarbone
- **21 stripes = Napoleon's victories** — almost certainly apocryphal; likely just the specification

## Why Creatives Wore It
Not mystical — practical:
- Cheap, durable, required no thought
- Bold enough to look deliberate without styling
- For people whose attention was on their work, not their clothing
- Picasso: "because I always wear a sailor's shirt" — identity, not fashion
- The absence of a style choice, photographed often enough, became a style

## Jean Paul Gaultier's Contribution
- Wore mariniere to first men's show (1978)
- Made it house signature from 1983 (Toy Boy collection)
- Key insight: separated the pattern from the garment
- Applied stripes to corsets, mesh, tailored suits, perfume bottles
- Identified what the Navy knew: horizontal lines create recognition, rhythm, visual presence at any distance
- Where Picasso wore it instinctively, Gaultier studied why it worked

## Heritage Manufacturers
- **Saint James** (Normandy, since 1889) — still produces on original principles
- **Armor Lux** (Quimper, since 1938) — faithful reproductions
- Construction (dense tubular knit) is what separates a mariniere from a striped shirt
