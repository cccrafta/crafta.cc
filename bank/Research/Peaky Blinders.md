## Overview

*Peaky Blinders* (BBC, 2013–2022) is set in Birmingham from 1919 onward, following the Shelby family gang in the aftermath of WWI. The show's costume design occupies a specific and contested space: it is broadly grounded in post-war British working-class dress but deliberately heightened for dramatic effect. The result produced one of the most influential television aesthetics in recent menswear history — not because it was historically accurate, but because it identified something latent in the garments and made it visible.

The core tension in the show's fashion is between what working-class men in Birmingham actually wore — one suit, one overcoat, one pair of boots, rarely cleaned — and what the show depicts: tailored three-piece suits, bespoke overcoats with velvet collars, precisely maintained flat caps. The show chose aspiration over documentary. And aspiration, it turns out, is what drives menswear.

## Historical Reality vs. the Show

### The Real Gang
The Peaky Blinders gang operated from the 1880s to approximately the 1910s — roughly 10-20 years *before* the show's setting. By the 1920s, the original gang had largely retreated to the West Midlands countryside, replaced in Birmingham by the Birmingham Boys. The razor-blade-in-cap detail is almost certainly a myth: Gillette didn't open its first London office until 1905, and disposable blades weren't widely sold in Britain until around 1910, by which point the original gang had declined. The name "Peaky" likely refers to the peaked brim of the cap. "Blinder" is Birmingham slang for someone strikingly stylish — someone with a "blinding" look.

### What Working-Class Birmingham Men Actually Wore (1919)
Post-WWI working-class men in Small Heath would typically have owned:
- One pre-made suit (not bespoke, not fitted, off the rack in heavy wool or tweed)
- One overcoat
- One pair of boots — lace-up, pointed toe for dress; thick-soled and round-toed for work
- A flat cap or newsboy cap (standard working-class headwear; the bowler hat was for foremen and managers)
- Detachable collars worn on Sundays, saved from daily wear
- Thin vertical-striped dress shirts with stiff white club collars

Historical photographs from Small Heath circa 1917 (Birmingham Archives & Heritage) show that working-class men of the area were "deliberately and self-consciously stylish" — silk neck scarves, smart tweed or wool jackets, flat caps. There was pride in dress, even within material constraint. The show captures the psychology accurately even as it exaggerates the execution.

## The Costume — What Stephanie Collie Actually Made

**Stephanie Collie** designed Season 1. Her research included Getty Images archives, criminal mug shots from the period, and the book *Crooks Like Us* by Peter Doyle (originally about Australian convicts — she drew hairstyle inspiration from it). She was explicit: "we heighten things to make them more relatable."

**Key construction decisions:**

- **Tommy Shelby's suits** — bespoke, made by Keith Watson (Savile Row-trained). All others hired from Angels and Cosprop, then tailored with narrower trouser legs.
- **Fabrics** — tweed primary; also moleskin and velvet. Authentic tweed sourced from Scottish factories. Period-appropriate buttons and cufflinks sourced from vintage fairs.
- **Trouser legs** — deliberately shortened, trouser hems lifted slightly above historical accuracy
- **Stiff collars** — Collie insisted on tight shirt collars worn close to the neck, despite actor discomfort
- **Overcoats** — some vintage originals, others custom-made. Inspired by Sergio Leone's Western aesthetic — "long, floating coats tailored lighter so they moved dramatically"
- **Silhouette** — post-WWI rationing informed the shorter pants and fitted cuts; the long coat counterbalances

**Season 2+ (Alison McCosh)** — researched actual period photographs from Birmingham to maintain foundational authenticity while adapting for drama.

## The Key Garments

### The Three-Piece Suit
The centre of the Peaky Blinders look. Charcoal herringbone tweed, high-waisted flat-front trousers, self-covered buttons on jacket and waistcoat. In Tommy's wardrobe specifically:
- **Jacket**: Single-breasted 2-button, notch lapels, welted breast pocket, straight flapped hip pockets, 2-button cuffs, ventless back
- **Waistcoat**: Single-breasted 6-button, notched bottom, welted hip pockets, adjustable rear strap
- **Trousers**: High-rise, flat front, frogmouth front pockets, straight leg, plain hem with short break

Palette: near-black, charcoal, dove grey, chocolate brown, navy. Occasionally muted herringbone or chalk stripe. Nothing shouts.

### The Overcoat
Long, structured, heavy — military in bearing. Key details:
- Heavy wool or tweed in black, charcoal, or dark navy
- Velvet half collar — the detail that elevates it from workwear to authority
- Wide peak lapels
- Worn draped, not buttoned — one button at the waist at most. A deliberate choice: a fully buttoned overcoat reads as functional; draped reads as theatrical and powerful
- Red or burgundy silk lining visible at lapels
- Long single vent at back
- Character evolution: Tommy is the only Blinder who wears a velvet-collared overcoat, visually setting him apart from the gang

The inspiration was explicitly the *Leone Western* — specifically the Leone treatment of a long coat as an extension of character rather than a practical garment.

### The Club Collar Shirt
The collar that disappeared and the show brought back. Detachable stiff white club collar, held with a gold stud. Collarless body beneath. Tommy wears pale blue cotton shirts in Season 1 with plain front fastenings and contrasting white single cuffs. The club collar was economically motivated — only the collar needed frequent laundering — and carries visual authority: formal without a tie, utilitarian without looking underdressed.

The collar says: this man has been somewhere. Post-WWI, men who had worn military uniforms for years returned with an unconscious formality in how they dressed. The club collar shirt is a civilian uniform, not a costume.

### The Flat Cap / Newsboy Cap
The cap is the gang's badge — not the gang's weapon. Different characters wear different styles:
- Tommy: newsboy/baker boy cap (8-panel, soft and wide)
- Arthur: flatter, more traditional flat cap
- John: peaked variant

The choice is deliberate and ideologically loaded. As the Shelbys climb — from gang to business to politics — they can afford and are expected to wear fedoras and bowlers. They refuse. The cap is a statement of origin, not circumstance. It says: we came from here, and we haven't forgotten it, and you should be afraid of that.

Historically, the newsboy cap had been introduced to England at the end of the 16th century as mandatory headwear, became working-class by the Industrial Revolution. The 8-panel construction with a button at the centre top distinguishes it from the simpler flat cap (single piece of fabric). The show blurs the distinction but generally favours the newsboy form for the Shelbys.

## The Zeitgeist — What the Clothes Are Actually About

The show is set in 1919. The war has just ended. Men who had been told what to wear, how to stand, when to move, and who to kill for four years have come home to unemployment, poverty, and a country that has no plan for them. The shell shock is real (Tommy has it), the violence is channelled rather than resolved, and the question of what a man *is* when the war is over sits beneath every scene.

The clothes answer that question without saying it. The three-piece suit is armour. The overcoat is a coat of arms. The cap is a flag. These men are not dressing for comfort or convention — they are constructing identities from the cloth available to them, in a city where the only other option is to be swallowed by the factory.

This is what makes the show's fashion legible to contemporary audiences even though it's a hundred years removed. The post-WWI period is a recognizable modern feeling: the world changed, the old order broke, and men are navigating between the person the system made them and the person they want to be. The clothes are the negotiation.

## What the Show Got Right (Deliberately)
- Tweed as the primary fabric — historically correct and texturally expressive
- High-waisted, shorter-leg trousers — reflects post-war rationing of material
- Club collar shirts — historically accurate to the period
- Cap as working-class identity marker vs. fedora/bowler as aspirational marker
- The boxy, layered silhouette — heavy coat over heavy suit reflects the Birmingham climate and the labour-class body

## What the Show Stylized
- Suits are too well-fitted, too consistently clean, too bespoke for actual working-class gang members
- The velvet collar overcoat is aristocratic, not working-class
- Razor blades in caps — almost certainly a myth
- The real gang operated in the 1890s-1910s, not the 1920s — the show advances the timeline significantly

## Contemporary Impact

*Peaky Blinders* premiered in 2013 and ran until 2022. Its fashion impact on menswear was direct and measurable:
- **Flat cap sales surged** repeatedly throughout the show's run
- **Tweed suit demand increased** — the show created a generation of men who knew what tweed was and wanted to wear it
- **Club collar revival** — the collar appeared in contemporary collections (Gucci, Ralph Lauren incorporated heritage tailoring silhouettes)
- **Three-piece suit** interest grew — driven partly by the show's influence
- The show's creator Steven Knight started **Garrison Tailors**, a clothing line named after the pub
- Viberg collaborated with Division Road on a Peaky Blinders-inspired derby boot (2018)
- NSS Magazine documented the flat cap's return as directly linked to the show's aesthetic alongside a 1990s nostalgia wave

Simon Crompton (Permanent Style) noted that collar styles from *Mad Men*, *Boardwalk Empire*, and *Peaky Blinders* periodically make collars fashionable again — but they "never stop appearing 'period' to most people." The heritage is real; the barrier is cultural not sartorial.
