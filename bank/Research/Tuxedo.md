## Overview
The tuxedo (dinner jacket in Britain, le smoking in France) was born from Edward VII wanting to eat dinner without wearing a tailcoat. What followed is 160 years of formality negotiating with comfort, with every detail serving a functional purpose most wearers never notice.

## Historical Timeline

### The Smoking Jacket Origin
- **1850s** — Crimean War popularises Turkish tobacco in Britain. Smoking jackets designed to absorb cigar smoke and protect clothing. Made from velvet/silk with shawl collar. [^1]
- Country squires began requesting the smoking jacket in black wool with tailcoat trimmings, dignified enough for the dining room. [^2]

### Birth of the Dinner Jacket
- **1865** — Prince of Wales (future Edward VII) orders a midnight blue silk smoking jacket with matching trousers from Henry Poole & Co., Savile Row, for informal dinners at Sandringham. Short, ventless, no tails — retained formal faced lapels. [^3]
- Henry Poole — first bespoke house on Savile Row. Simon Cundey (current MD): "Edward VII and Henry Poole designed the dinner suit between them." [^3]
- Described as "an invention born of supreme laziness and whimsy" — survived only through royal endorsement. [^4]

### Crossing the Atlantic
- **1886** — Two competing stories: [^4] [^5]
  1. **James Brown Potter** — member of the Tuxedo Club, New York. Met Edward VII, dined at Sandringham. Poole made him a short midnight blue evening coat. Potter wore it at the Tuxedo Club.
  2. **Griswold Lorillard** (tobacco heir) wore "tail-less" dinner jackets to a ball at the Tuxedo Club in Tuxedo Park, NY.
- Took the club's name: "tuxedo." Known as "dinner jacket" in Britain, "le smoking" in France. [^1]
- Initially rejected by American high society as improper. [^5]

### Edwardian Establishment
- **1901-1914** — Black waistcoat and black bow tie become convention, establishing current black tie / white tie distinction. [^6]
- Edward VII credited with "creating sportswear as a separate class of clothing, bridging formal and informal." [^6]

## Construction & Materials

### Fabric
- **Barathea** — classic British choice. Satin-group weave of worsted wool. Dense, deep drape, rich black. The norm for British tailors. [^7]
- **Wool/mohair blends** — mohair adds crispness, lustre, shape-holding. Even at 14-16%, dramatically improves drape and wrinkle resistance. [^7] [^8]
- **Weight:** 9-10oz (300-340 g/m²) — most venues heated, lighter is practical. [^7]
- **Colour:** Midnight blue preferred over black — black reads brownish under artificial light, midnight blue appears truly black. Prince of Wales championed this in the 1920s. [^7] [^9]
- **Shine:** Body cloth must be matte — lapel facings provide lustre. The contrast IS the design. [^7]

### Construction Details
- **Lapel facings:** Silk satin (lustrous) or grosgrain (ribbed, subtler). Grosgrain preferred in England, associated with custom tailoring. [^7] [^10]
- **Trouser stripe:** Grosgrain or satin down outside leg — echoes the lapel, creates visual continuity. [^10]
- **Waistband:** Higher than day trousers. No belt loops. Side adjusters — waist covered by cummerbund or waistcoat. [^10]
- **Canvas:** Body canvas full length, horsehair at top, domette over. Two darts each side for waist shape. [^11]
- **Venting:** Centre vent unacceptable — opens when reaching into pockets. Double vent or ventless. [^10]
- **Buttonholes:** Slim, neat, handmade on best examples. Grosgrain facing hand-attached. [^11]

### Why the Fabric Matters
The dinner jacket operates in controlled environments — candlelit rooms, heated venues, long evenings. The fabric must be lightweight for hours of wear (9-10oz) [^7], hold shape without wrinkling (mohair) [^8], produce true colour under artificial light (midnight blue) [^7], provide matte ground for silk facings to catch light [^7], and drape cleanly seated yet recover standing (barathea) [^7].

## Cultural Moments & Zeitgeist

### The Jazz Age (1920s) — Elevation
- The Prince of Wales regularly chose the dinner jacket over the tailcoat, elevating it to standard. [^9]
- Championed midnight blue — richer under artificial light than black. [^9]
- Vogue lamented the "decline of masculine elegance" as the tuxedo displaced the tailcoat. [^9]

### The Golden Age (1930s) — Hollywood & Democratisation
- Widely regarded as the peak of men's formal dressing. [^12]
- **Fred Astaire's tailcoats** were engineered for dance: Kilgour made his Top Hat (1935) suit with enlarged sleevehead and exaggerated rope shoulder for arm movement, rounded waistcoat edges to clear raised legs. [^13]
- Astaire danced up and down the hall during fittings at Anderson & Sheppard to test that his collar stayed put and nothing pulled. [^14]
- Astaire threw new suits against walls to break their stiffness: "I often take a brand-new suit or hat and throw it up against the wall a few times to get that stiff, square newness out of it." [^14]
- DB and white summer versions emerged. Cummerbund and turndown-collar shirt became standard. [^12]
- **Democratisation:** Mass-produced RTW tuxedos (S. Rudofker's Sons, Philadelphia). Tuxedo rental became an option. [^12]
- The American prom descended from debutante balls in the 1920s, popularised in the 1930s — trading youthful clothes for adult evening wear. [^12]

### The Academy Awards — The Tuxedo's Stage
- **1929:** First ceremony — a small dinner, already glamorous. [^15]
- **1953:** First televised. [^15]
- **1964:** Academy extended the red carpet, began focusing broadcasts on arrivals — the tuxedo became a global spectacle. [^15]
- **1969:** Gower Champion established formal dress code — tuxedos for men, gowns for women. [^15]
- **2019:** Billy Porter's tuxedo gown merged male tuxedo with female gown, sparking conversations about gender in formal dress. [^15]
- **2026:** "A red carpet slowly loosening its grip on strict tradition." [^16]

### YSL's Le Smoking (1966) — Gender Expansion
- Fall/winter 1966 "Pop Art" collection, August 1966. [^17]
- Inspired by 1933 photograph of Marlene Dietrich. Saint Laurent: "A woman dressed as a man must be at the height of femininity." [^17]
- Not a copy — narrowed waistline, adjusted pants, subtler collar curve. [^17]
- Scandalous: Nan Kempner turned away from La Côte Basque in New York. She reportedly removed the trousers and re-entered wearing just the jacket as a mini dress. [^18]
- Only one haute couture version sold initially — Rive Gauche RTW was the success. [^17]
- Saint Laurent designed over 200 variations until retirement in 2002. [^17]
- Helmut Newton's 1975 Rue Aubriot photograph for French Vogue made the look immortal. [^18]

### WWII — Interruption
- Formal clothing mothballed during wartime. Post-war, black tie became special-occasion only. [^5]
- Lost patrician exclusivity as the 1950s middle class adopted it. [^5]

### The Decline — Casualisation
- 1960s counterculture targeted the suit as conformity. [^19]
- Silicon Valley (1970s+): by 1990, ties disappeared from tech. [^19]
- Peter Thiel (2014): "Never invest in a tech CEO that wears a suit." [^19]
- COVID-19 normalised remote attire permanently. [^19]

### Did the Tuxedo Adapt?
- Didn't die — narrowed its context. Standard evening wear → special-occasion → ceremonial/gala only. [unsourced — synthesis]
- Adapted through relaxation: midnight blue replacing black, open collar replacing bow tie. [unsourced — synthesis]
- Le Smoking expanded its gender. [^17]
- Tuxedo DNA dispersed into "smart evening" — silk-lapel blazers, grosgrain-trimmed trousers with knitwear. [unsourced — synthesis]

## Contemporary Makers

### Bespoke
- **Henry Poole & Co.** (Savile Row, since 1806) — the originator, still produces. [^3]
- **Cifonelli** (Paris) — roped shoulder (grandfather's invention), 6x1 DB style. [^20]
- **Richard Anderson** (Savile Row) — understated, narrow shoulder. [^11]
- **Anderson & Sheppard** — soft drape cut, Astaire's tailor. [^14]
- **Huntsman** — dramatic one-button cuts. [^20]

### RTW / MTM
- **Thom Sweeney** — sleek tapered trousers. [^20]
- **Richard James** (Savile Row) — lightweight wool/mohair, softer cut. [^20]

## The "Workwear" Argument
The tuxedo has a specific job: make the wearer appropriate for a formal occasion while allowing hours of movement, eating, and socialising. Every detail solves a functional problem. Astaire proved this — he danced in his dinner clothes because they needed to perform. [^14] The tuxedo is equipment for a specific environment, the way a safari jacket is equipment for the bush.
