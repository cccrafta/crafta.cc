## Overview
The innerwear category — what goes directly against skin or as the first visible layer under a jacket — is surprisingly deep. It spans from the purely functional (undershirts, thermals) to the stylistic (henleys, Breton stripes) to the hybrid (waffle knits that insulate AND look good exposed). Each type serves different needs across style, function, comfort, and durability.

## The Innerwear Categories

### 1. The White T-Shirt
- The foundation of casual layering
- Originally military underwear (US Navy, 1913)
- Works under everything: denim jackets, leather, field jackets
- Key quality factors: cotton weight (5-7oz ideal), tubular vs side-seam construction, collar ribbing
- Longevity: loopwheeled versions hold shape far longer than cut-and-sew

### 2. The Henley
- Named after Henley-on-Thames, home of the Royal Regatta since 1839
- Originally 19th-century English undergarments ("undervests")
- 2-3 button placket adds visual interest without a collar
- Precursor to the modern t-shirt — Cooper Underwear Co. removed the buttons ~1904
- Modern status: workwear essential, Hollywood adoption (Drive, John Wick)
- Materials: cotton (standard), merino wool (performance), waffle knit (thermal)
- Heritage brands: Merz b. Schwanen (loopwheeled, German revival), The Real McCoys, Iron Heart

### 3. The Waffle Thermal
- Waffle knit creates air pockets that trap insulating warmth
- Originally military-issue base layer
- 100% cotton (traditional) vs merino wool (performance) vs blends (durability)
- Japanese makers lead quality: UES (13oz big waffle), Iron Heart, Loop & Weft, Warehouse
- Can be worn as standalone in mild weather or as true base layer in cold
- Key quality: tight knit density, longer cotton fibers = less pilling

### 4. The Oxford / Chambray / Flannel Shirt
- The "proper" layer under a jacket when a collar is needed
- Oxford cloth button-down (OCBD): the all-rounder
- Chambray: lighter weight, indigo casual alternative
- Flannel: warmth + softness, workwear register
- Creates visual frame around the face via collar

### 5. The Breton Stripe
- Naval origin (French Navy, 1858)
- Strong visual identity without being loud
- Works under open jackets for pattern interest
- Cotton jersey, holds up to repeated washing

### 6. The Sweatshirt / Crew Neck
- Heavyweight layering piece
- Loopwheeled versions from Wakayama maintain shape
- Japanese heritage brands apply indigo, kakishibu dyeing to sweatshirts
- Best under roomier jackets (field jackets, chore coats) — too bulky under fitted leather

## Style Considerations
- White/grey tees + leather jacket = timeless casual
- Henley + denim jacket = workwear elevated
- OCBD + blazer = smart casual
- Breton stripe + any jacket = effortless character
- Collar vs no collar changes the entire register of the outfit
- Thinnest layers closest to body, bulk increases outward

## Functionality
- Cotton: breathable, absorbs moisture, slow to dry
- Merino wool: wicks moisture, temperature regulating, odor resistant
- Waffle knit: traps air = insulation, works year-round depending on weight
- Linen: fastest drying, coolest, but wrinkles (summer only)

## Durability / Longevity
- Loopwheeled knitting = maintains shape longest (low-tension process)
- Fiber length determines pilling resistance (longer = better)
- Tight knit gauge > loose gauge for longevity
- Tubular construction (no side seams) = fewer stress points
- Japanese-made bases (Iron Heart, UES, Loop & Weft) emphasize construction quality
