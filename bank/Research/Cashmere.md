## Overview

Cashmere is the fine undercoat of the Capra hircus laniger goat — an animal that evolved in some of the world's harshest climates specifically to survive temperatures of -40°C. The fiber that keeps the animal alive is, by direct consequence, the finest natural insulating material available to textile makers. Every property that makes cashmere expensive — its fineness (14–19 microns), its warmth-to-weight ratio (three times that of standard wool), its scarcity (150–200 grams per goat per year) — is an accident of survival rather than breeding for luxury. The goat did not evolve to produce a luxury product. It evolved to stay alive. The fiber is a by-product of that necessity.

## The Fiber — What It Is

Cashmere comes from the dense, fine undercoat grown by Capra hircus laniger goats during winter. The goat grows two coats: a coarse outer "guard hair" (30–75 microns) that repels water and wind, and a fine inner down (14–19 microns) that provides thermal insulation. Only the inner down is cashmere. The guard hair is used for brushes and other non-textile applications.

**Why so fine:** The goat lives at altitude — the Changthang region of Ladakh, the Tibetan plateau, Mongolian steppe. Temperatures reach -40°C. Natural selection favored animals with the finest, most insulating undercoats. The fiber is fine because survival demanded it. [^1]

**Fiber specifications:**
- Average diameter: 14–19 microns (FTC standard: must not exceed 19µm average, no more than 3% of fibers above 30µm) [^1]
- Changthangi goats of Himalayan highlands: 12–13 microns (finest) [^1]
- Chinese/Mongolian commercial production: 14.5–16.5 microns [^1]
- Iranian goats: 17.5–19.5 microns (coarser end of acceptable range) [^1]
- Staple length: typically 40–60mm (shorter than merino's 50–120mm) [^5]
- Annual yield per goat: 150–200 grams of raw fiber [^1]
- Global annual production: approximately 6,500 metric tons of pure cashmere vs. 2 million metric tons of sheep's wool [^1]

**One sweater requires** the annual production of 3–4 goats. An overcoat: 30–40 goats. [^1]

## Fiber Structure — What Makes It Different

Cashmere is not merely fine wool. Its properties diverge from wool at the structural level:

**Surface scales:** Wool has prominent, overlapping cuticle scales with a waxy coating. Cashmere has fewer, flatter, smoother scales. This is why cashmere doesn't itch against skin — the scales don't catch. It is also why cashmere felts less aggressively than wool in washing. [^5]

**Crimp:** Merino wool has well-defined, high-frequency crimp that creates elasticity and resilience. Cashmere has irregular, less-defined crimp — which is why it drapes and lofts rather than springs back. The irregular crimp also means cashmere has lower elasticity than wool, which contributes to pilling and stretching over time. [^5]

**Hollow fiber chambers:** Each cashmere fiber has air chambers inside. These chambers trap still air — the mechanism that makes cashmere three times warmer than standard wool at the same weight. The same chambers allow moisture vapor to pass through (breathability) while retaining body heat. A 200-gram cashmere sweater provides equivalent warmth to a 600-gram wool jumper. [^5]

**Bending stiffness:** Bending stiffness is proportional to the fourth power of fiber diameter. A small reduction in microns = massive increase in fabric flexibility and drape. This is why cashmere drapes where wool structures. [^5]

**Hygroscopicity:** Cashmere absorbs over 15% of its dry weight in moisture from the air, holding it inside the fiber rather than on the skin. This is why cashmere regulates temperature across a wider range than most textiles — it absorbs sweat vapor rather than letting it accumulate on the surface. [^5]

## Harvesting — The Scarcity Problem

Unlike sheep's wool (shorn), cashmere is harvested by hand-combing during the spring moulting season (March to May in the Northern Hemisphere). The goat naturally sheds its undercoat; the combing collects the mixed fiber before it's lost to wind and terrain.

**The window is narrow** — harvesting too early stresses the animal, too late loses fiber. A few weeks per year, per goat, 150–200 grams of raw mixed fiber. Processing removes 50% of that: de-hairing separates the cashmere from guard hairs. High-quality garment fiber must contain less than 1% coarse hair; coating fabrics less than 5%. [^1]

**Combing vs. shearing:** Combed cashmere yields a higher percentage of pure cashmere from the mixed fleece because it removes less guard hair than shearing. Most high-grade cashmere is combed. The lower the guard hair contamination, the higher the price. [^1]

## From Kashmir to Europe — The Garment History

The Kashmir shawl is where cashmere meets garment history. For centuries, the finest pashmina shawls were reserved for Mughal aristocracy — fineness as a mark of nobility. The shawls required months of hand-weaving by skilled Kashmiri craftsmen; the most intricate took years. They were not garments in the Western sense; they were records of craft.

Napoleon's Egyptian campaign (1798–1801) brought the first significant quantities of Kashmir shawls to Paris. General Bonaparte sent one to Paris from Egypt; it created an immediate sensation. Empress Joséphine — who initially wrote that she found them "hideous" — became the most consequential collector of cashmere in Western fashion history. She eventually owned 300–400 shawls. Her public appearances wearing them drove demand across France and into Britain. [^2]

**Why shawls suited post-Revolutionary fashion:** After the French Revolution, European women abandoned the heavy brocade dresses of the 18th century for simple muslin in empire-waist cuts. Shoulders were exposed. The Kashmir shawl solved a thermal and aesthetic problem simultaneously: warmth, color, and the suggestion of the Orient against the austerity of republican fashion. [^2]

**Industrial imitation:** By the 1780s, Norwich, Edinburgh, and Lyon were producing imitation Kashmir shawls. William-Louis Ternaux began production at Reims in 1799 using Spanish merino, then transitioned to actual cashmere by 1811. Paisley, Scotland became a center for imitation shawl production — the paisley pattern derives from Kashmiri buta motifs, filtered through European industrial looms. [^2]

**The de-hairing revolution:** In the 1870s, Joseph Dawson (a Yorkshire mill-worker) invented an efficient mechanical process for separating cashmere undercoat from guard hair — inspired by an overland journey through India. This made cashmere viable at industrial scale. Dawson's company later became a conglomerate that acquired both Pringle of Scotland and Ballantyne. [^3]

## Scotland — The Manufacturing Story

By the late 1700s, Hawick (pronounced "Hoyk") on the River Teviot in the Scottish Borders was organized around hosiery. The pure, fast-flowing Border rivers powered the mills. By the 1870s, 250 woollen mills clustered around Hawick and the Scottish Borders, representing over 40% of Scotland's factory labour force.

Cashmere arrived as a raw material in the late 19th century. The Scottish mills possessed the skills, the water chemistry, and the manufacturing infrastructure to process it. Hawick became the global center for cashmere knitwear manufacture — shipping to New York, Paris, Milan, and the great couture houses, including Coco Chanel.

**The decline and the survivors:** Italian and Chinese competition, combined with insufficient government support, closed most of the historic mills. Pringle's Hawick plant closed in 2008. Caerlee Mill (maker of Ballantyne) was shuttered and demolished. Today:
- **William Lockie** (est. 1874): still manufacturing in Hawick; specialist in Scottish cuts and traditional knitwear [^4]
- **Johnstons of Elgin** (est. 1797): the only Scottish cashmere company that controls the entire process from raw fiber to finished garment; also supplies yarn to major fashion houses [^4]
- **Barrie Knitwear** (est. 1903): acquired by Chanel in 2012 through its Métiers d'Art initiative; still hand-assessing each garment's washing individually; the only manufacturer to have its own training school for preserving traditional techniques; produces intarsia work for Chanel collections [^4]

## Quality — What Separates Good from Bad Cashmere

**Micron and fiber length:** The primary quality determinants. Finer microns = softer hand. Longer staple = more durable (shorter staples pill faster because the loose fiber ends work free). Mongolian cashmere is generally considered finest for commercial production (14–16 microns); Chinese Inner Mongolian cashmere 15–18 microns; Afghan and Iranian coarser. [^6]

**Ply count:** Two-ply is a reliable durability indicator. Single-ply is cheaper to produce (less yarn required), softer initially but shorter-lived. Quality makers rarely do single-ply. Four-ply represents significant investment in material and produces garments that last decades. Permanent Style documented falling in love with four-ply cashmere — "a quality no one really sells anymore." [^6]

**Knit density:** Loosely knit cashmere (common in mass-market pieces) uses less yarn and can be chemically softened to feel luxurious immediately. Dense knitting holds its shape, pills less, and ages better. Chemical softeners wash out over time, revealing the looseness underneath. [^6]

**The pilling problem:** Cashmere's shorter staple length (vs. merino) means loose fiber ends are more accessible at the surface. Any friction — under the arm, at cuffs, against a bag strap — causes fibers to tangle into pills. Pills are not a sign of failure; they are a sign the garment is alive. High-quality cashmere pills initially, then stabilizes as the loose surface fibers work out. Cheap cashmere continues to pill until the garment thins. Brunello Cucinelli reportedly shows almost no pilling even under the arm. Loro Piana uses longer, stable cashmere to reduce it. [^6]

**The Scottish water advantage:** Scottish mills cite the soft, mineral-poor water from Border rivers as a factor in finishing quality — the water doesn't leave residue in the fiber, and washing is gentler. Barrie Knitwear judges each garment's washing individually rather than batch-processing. [^4]

## What Cashmere Does to a Garment

Cashmere's fiber properties determine its garment behavior precisely:

**Drape:** The fine diameter, low bending stiffness, and irregular crimp produce a fabric that falls against the body rather than holding away from it. A cashmere overcoat drapes where wool structures. Simon Crompton (Permanent Style) noted a Cifonelli cashmere overcoat "frustratingly soft" — it rarely holds a clean line, even after nine years. This is not a defect; it is a property. [^6]

**Warmth without weight:** Hollow fiber chambers trap still air. A cashmere sweater at 200 grams provides the insulation of 600 grams of wool. This is why cashmere is viable for garments where mass matters — the overcoat, the travel layer, the garment worn on the body all day. [^5]

**Temperature regulation:** The hygroscopic fiber absorbs body moisture vapor rather than letting it sit on the skin. The hollow chambers both retain heat and, via evaporation, allow excess heat to escape. Cashmere works across a wider temperature range than almost any other natural fiber. [^5]

**Patina:** Cashmere does not age neutrally. A well-used cashmere sweater develops a soft bloom at the surface as fibers work out and interlock. The pills of the early years give way to a settled surface with more body than when new. A cardigan documented by Permanent Style — knitted in 2009, given by Michael Drake — was sent to Cashmere Circle for a full revive (washing, de-pilling, repair) at £87 after 15+ years. It then looked new. The fiber is repairable in a way synthetics are not. [^6]

## What Publications Are Saying

**Permanent Style:**
- Best cashmere comes from Mongolia; Chinese cashmere is cheaper and harder to distinguish by eye alone [^6]
- For durability, seek twill weave over hopsack or plain at 340g/11oz or above [^6]
- Chinese cashmere 15–16 microns, Mongolian 16.2–18 microns (counterintuitively, some Mongolian grades are coarser than Chinese due to breed variation) [^6]
- Four-ply is a vanishing quality standard — warmer, heavier, dramatically longer-lasting [^6]
- Cheap cashmere is knitted loosely with chemical softener — feels better than it is [^6]

**Heddels:**
- Cashmere fineness is similar to superfine merino (~18 microns); its value is luxury hand feel, not technical superiority over merino [^7]
- One goat = 150 grams/year; this explains the price [^7]
- Cashmere has similar resilience to sheep's wool but finer fibers make it more delicate [^7]

**Put This On:**
- William Lockie remains one of the survivors of authentic Scottish cashmere manufacturing; a benchmark for traditional craft [^4]

## Gaps — What Hasn't Been Written

- The tension between Mongolian pastoral production methods and Chinese industrial-scale cashmere farming — environmental overgrazing by cashmere goats is a serious grassland degradation issue in both countries, largely absent from fashion coverage
- The Kashmiri pashmina craft in crisis: political instability in Kashmir, difficulty sourcing authentic pashmina at 12–13 microns, and near-impossible quality verification
- What four-ply cashmere actually means for the maker — why it almost disappeared commercially, which mills still produce it, who specifies it
- The relationship between cashmere overcoat and tailoring weight — why most cashmere coats fail at the line while wool excels
