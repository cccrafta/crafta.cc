## Overview
The CPO (Chief Petty Officer) shirt is a heavyweight wool overshirt introduced by the U.S. Navy in the 1930s. Its defining quality is ambiguity — too heavy for a shirt, too light for a jacket — which makes it uniquely functional in the layering gap between the two.

## Historical Timeline
- **1930s** — U.S. Navy introduces the CPO as "Shirt, Wool, Blue" for Chief Petty Officers
- **1930s-1940s** — Original design: single chest pocket with scalloped flap, anchor-embossed buttons
- **Late 1940s** — Second chest pocket added
- **Late 1960s** — Navy phases out 100% wool, CPO fades from military issue
- **Post-military** — Enters civilian wardrobes through surplus channels
- **2010s-present** — Resurgence via heritage brands

## Key Construction Details
- **Fabric:** Navy melton wool — dense, felted, heavy enough to cut wind
- **Pockets:** Originally one chest pocket with scalloped flap; dual pocket from late 1940s
- **Buttons:** Anchor-embossed, tonal
- **Construction:** No lining, no insulation, no epaulettes — deliberately spare
- **Hem:** Straight (not curved like a dress shirt)
- **Cut:** Roomy enough to layer over knits and thermals

## The In-Between Quality
- A sweater provides warmth but no structure
- A jacket provides structure but commits to outerwear
- The CPO splits the difference: structured enough as a light jacket, soft enough to layer under a coat
- Identity depends on maintaining this ambiguity — add a lining or stiffen the shoulder and it becomes something else

## Contemporary Makers
- **Schott** — classic anchor buttons, streamlined cut
- **Freenote Cloth** — Midway CPO in Japanese melton wool
- **Iron Heart** — heavyweight interpretations
- **Engineered Garments** — evolved field shirt jacket versions
- **Kapital** — cotton Navy CPO
- Each preserves: shirt collar, patch pockets, weight that exceeds a shirt
