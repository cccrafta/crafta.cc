## Overview
The safari jacket traces from Boer War military uniforms through civilian expedition wear (Roosevelt, Hemingway) to haute couture (YSL) to contemporary menswear. Its four-pocket, belted architecture has proven remarkably resistant to alteration because each element depends on the others.

## Historical Timeline
- **~1900** — Boer War: British Army commissions lightweight khaki drill uniforms for South Africa
- **1909-1910** — Theodore Roosevelt's East African expedition, outfitted by Abercrombie & Fitch / Newland & Tarlton
- **1928** — Willis & Geiger Outfitters founded
- **1933** — Hemingway travels to Africa, adopts the bush jacket
- **1935** — "Safari suit" enters the Oxford English Dictionary
- **1936** — Hemingway commissions bespoke bush jacket from Willis & Geiger (centre vent, sleeve pocket for glasses, quilted recoil pad, shell loops)
- **1939** — Abercrombie & Fitch advertises safari jackets wholesale
- **1940** — Douglas Fairbanks Jr. in Safari (film)
- **1952** — Gregory Peck in The Snows of Kilimanjaro
- **1953** — Clark Gable in Mogambo
- **1968** — YSL's Saharienne collection
- **1970s** — Banana Republic (original high-end safari-themed outfitter) revives the style
- **Present** — Bryceland's, The Armoury, Grenfell, Westley Richards, Private White V.C., Kaptain Sunshine, Fortela

## Key Design Features
- Four bellows pockets (two chest for quick access, two lower for bulkier items)
- Self-fabric belt (manages weight of loaded pockets)
- Broad collar (sun/wind protection, frames face)
- Cotton drill or poplin (breathable, thorn-resistant)
- Pale coloring (reflects sunlight, hides dust)
- Epaulettes (military origin, rank display)

## Hemingway's Bespoke Jacket (Willis & Geiger, 1936)
Specific features commissioned from field experience:
- Centre vent for movement
- Sleeve pocket sized for glasses
- Quilted recoil pad at shoulder
- Shotgun shell loops in lining
- Full bellows pockets upper and lower

Willis & Geiger also outfitted: Charles Lindbergh, Amelia Earhart, Edmund Hillary

## Heritage Brands — Lineage Makers

These are makers whose safari jackets descend from a direct line of outfitting explorers, soldiers, or hunters. They didn't adopt the form — they helped create it.

### Willis & Geiger Outfitters (1928–1977/1987)
- Founded by Ben Willis (who developed clothing for his own Arctic explorations from 1902) and marketer Howard W. Geiger in 1928 [^4]
- Previously sold through Abercrombie & Fitch, who outfitted Teddy Roosevelt with Willis-designed clothing [^4]
- 1931: US defense contract for air force pilot jackets [^4]
- Outfitted: Roald Amundsen, Charles Lindbergh, Amelia Earhart, Jacqueline Cochran, Edmund Hillary, Jim Fowler, the Flying Tigers [^4]
- 1936: Hemingway's bespoke bush jacket — cotton bush poplin, water buffalo horn buttons, centre vent, sleeve pocket for glasses, quilted recoil pad, shotgun shell loops, binocular pocket with lens cover compartment, gusseted sleeve ends, adjustable waistband against safari ants, covered cartridge loops [^4]
- 1977: When A&F went bankrupt, W&G was its largest unpaid creditor — went out of business [^4]
- Revived by former A&F employee Burt Avedon, sold to VF Corporation (1986, shut US manufacturing), then Laura Ashley (1987), then Japanese market [^4]
- Today: defunct as a maker, but vintage pieces (especially the Hemingway Bush Jacket) are highly sought collector's items [^4]

### Westley Richards (Birmingham, est. 1812)
- Gunmaker and outfitter with 200+ years of history [^5]
- Inspired by former clients Hemingway and Stewart Granger [^5]
- **Selous Safari Jacket** (named after Frederick Courteney Selous): real horn buttons individually engraved with company name, four cartridge slots on right breast, small left-arm pocket for ear plugs/matches, action back for rifle/camera movement, working epaulettes, fully operational buttoned cuffs. Fabrics: Tencel/cotton/linen blends, or 100% linen. Made in Portugal. £695 [^6]
- **Bushveld Safari Jacket**: 100% cotton drill — tear-resistant, thorn-proof, tsetse-fly proof, dense weave. Designed for actual bush use, not fashion. £565 [^6]
- 2025 season: new lightweight Japanese cotton/linen/Tencel blend [^6]
- Their Expedition Safari Shirt described as having "that tough and reassuringly authentic look and feel of the old Willis & Geiger safari shirts" [^5]
- Blog (The Explora) covers safari clothing history from field-use perspective [^5]

### Huntsman (Savile Row)
- Made bespoke safari suits for Clark Gable, Ava Gardner, and Grace Kelly for *Mogambo* (1953) [^7]
- Structured, military-influenced Savile Row silhouette — heavier construction than most contemporaries [^7]
- Current collection under Creative Director Campbell Carey: ready-to-wear Seagreen Linen Herringbone (softer construction — thinner pads, lighter canvassing, soft sleeve head), herringbone pastels, navy seersucker [^7]
- Positions the safari jacket as refined "off-duty" style, not field equipment [^7]

### Holland & Holland
- Premium gunmaker with a Multi-Pocket Safari Jacket in lightweight safari cotton, four front pockets, top-stitched belt [^8]
- Like Westley Richards, a gunmaker-turned-outfitter — function-first

### Grenfell (England, est. 1923)
- Heritage outerwear maker, dedicated safari jacket collection [^8]
- All made in the UK

### Banana Republic (original, 1978–1988)
- Founded by Mel and Patricia Ziegler in 1978 as "Banana Republic Travel & Safari Clothing Company" [^9]
- Origin: Mel bought a British Burma jacket in a Sydney surplus store; Patricia altered it — leather elbow patches, wooden buttons replacing brass, removing pocket flaps — "taking it out of military context into safari style" [^9]
- Sold restyled military surplus with storytelling — e.g. "Short-Armed Spanish Paratrooper Shirts" (500 shirts bought at $1.50 each, sold at premium with a story attached) [^9]
- Stores designed as safari environments: bush planes from ceilings, thatched huts, jeeps [^9]
- 1983: Gap purchased; sales per sq ft peaked at $750 vs industry average $235 [^9]
- 1988: Zieglers resigned over "fundamental creative and cultural differences" — brand pivoted away from safari to generic upscale casual [^9]
- Relevant as the brand that briefly made the safari jacket accessible to the American mainstream — and whose decline proves the aesthetic requires authenticity to sustain

## Contemporary Makers

These are makers who adopted the safari form and reinterpreted it — each solving different problems with the original design.

### Bryceland's (Tokyo, est. ~2015)
- Founded by Australian Ethan Newton (@ethandesu) in Harajuku, Tokyo [^10]
- Safari Jacket in Smith Woollens Solaro — 100% wool, warm beige herringbone with red yarns on underside giving iridescent sheen [^10]
- Four bellowed pockets, inverted back pleat, self-tie belt, straight cut [^10]
- Also in HBT Olive [^10]
- Made by Ascot Chang (Hong Kong) [^10]
- Philosophy: "mix of tailoring and vintage, workwear and tailoring" — soft Italian make (Dalcuore, Orazio Luciano style) in English cloths [^10]
- "Better for the world if we only make what we need" — made-to-order focus [^10]
- Treats the safari jacket as craft, not costume — the Solaro choice elevates it to tailoring territory

### The Armoury (Hong Kong / New York)
- Safari jacket has been an essential for over a decade [^11]
- **Safari II**: evolved design — appears more minimal, but packed with functionality [^11]
- Waist pockets: enlarged with both gussets and bellows, plus two internal change pockets [^11]
- Chest pockets: elongated, rounded at corners, sized for passport/phone/sunglasses [^11]
- Angular collar based on vintage safari jackets, frames the face [^11]
- Very light canvassing, larger buttons, centre button creates V-shape silhouette [^11]
- Fabrics: Natural Irish Linen (heavier, reduces creasing), Brown Cotton Panama (structured, open weave for humidity) [^11]
- Made by Ascot Chang. MTO in thousands of fabrics [^11]
- Key innovation: bellows-and-gusset pockets that lie flat when empty — solving a problem originals never addressed

### Kaptain Sunshine (Japan)
- Designer Keita Kojima [^12]
- Belted Safari Light Jacket in Japanese-made Giza cotton — "takes the fuss out of traditional safari jackets" [^12]
- Also: Safari Shirt Jacket in silk/linen/cotton (lighter, shirt-weight approach) [^12]
- Inspired by American P41 military jacket, cotton/linen blends [^12]
- SS26 collaboration with Barbour (third collab) — maritime workwear foundation [^12]
- Distinctive for "fits that cannot be replicated elsewhere — some pieces look fitted yet relaxed simultaneously" [^12]

### Fortela (Italy)
- Carlo Safari Jacket: inspired by 1930s American Army models [^13]
- 100% wool twill (Manteco), unlined, waist belt, two large chest pockets with flaps, horn button closures [^13]
- Heddels noted: warm plaid wool "entirely changes the vibe — like a sophisticated movie star in the 1930s" [^13]
- Made in Italy

### Anglo-Italian (London)
- Garment-dyed safari jackets — lightweight, sloppy, much-loved appearance [^14]
- The opposite of most brands: where others mimic tailoring, Anglo-Italian goes deliberately casual [^14]
- Suited to jeans and a polo rather than a dress shirt [^14]
- Permanent Style SS18 Top 10: "Where most brands produce safari jackets that mimic tailoring, Anglo-Italian did the opposite" [^14]

### The Merchant Fox (England)
- Fox Safari Jacket, £755 [^15]
- Internal drawstring waist (instead of external belt), central rear vent [^15]
- "Won over by the fit, the collar shape, and the crisp lightness of the cloths, which help reduce the volume of the hip bellows-pockets" — Permanent Style [^15]

### Budd (London shirtmaker)
- Bespoke safari jacket commission documented by Permanent Style [^16]
- 265g army-green Irish linen from John England [^16]
- Multiple fittings: shoulders cut down ¼", chest and waist down ~½", length down 2" [^16]
- Key insight: going bespoke solves the safari jacket's core problem — "they often look rather colonial and middle-aged — partly the sand color and partly the fit" [^16]

### Private White V.C. (Manchester, est. ~1900s factory)
- Handcrafted in their own Manchester factory for 100+ years [^8]
- Linen Field Jacket 2.0: stone-washed Irish linen [^8]
- Also: olive sahariana with epaulettes, surplus of pockets [^8]

### Anderson & Sheppard (Savile Row)
- Bespoke safari jackets as part of broader repertoire [^8]
- Known for soft drape-cut — the softer end of the Savile Row spectrum, contrasting Huntsman's structured military approach [^8]

### Key insight from Permanent Style:
"The problem with safari jackets is that they often look rather colonial and middle-aged — partly the sand color and partly the fit. They rarely have waist suppression and tend to have large, blooming sleeves." Going bespoke (or choosing well-cut modern versions) solves this.

## Gaps
- Nobody has written about the tension between the jacket's colonial history and its modern appeal in a nuanced way
- The Hemingway bespoke details (glasses pocket, recoil pad) are rarely discussed — they show true equipment design thinking
- Japanese interpretations (Kaptain Sunshine, Bryceland's) treat it as craft rather than costume
