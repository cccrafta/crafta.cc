## Overview
The safari jacket traces from Boer War military uniforms through civilian expedition wear (Roosevelt, Hemingway) to haute couture (YSL) to contemporary menswear. Its four-pocket, belted architecture has proven remarkably resistant to alteration because each element depends on the others.

## Historical Timeline
- **~1900** — Boer War: British Army commissions lightweight khaki drill uniforms for South Africa
- **1909-1910** — Theodore Roosevelt's East African expedition, outfitted by Abercrombie & Fitch / Newland & Tarlton
- **1928** — Willis & Geiger Outfitters founded
- **1933** — Hemingway travels to Africa, adopts the bush jacket
- **1935** — "Safari suit" enters the Oxford English Dictionary
- **1936** — Hemingway commissions bespoke bush jacket from Willis & Geiger (centre vent, sleeve pocket for glasses, quilted recoil pad, shell loops)
- **1939** — Abercrombie & Fitch advertises safari jackets wholesale
- **1940** — Douglas Fairbanks Jr. in Safari (film)
- **1952** — Gregory Peck in The Snows of Kilimanjaro
- **1953** — Clark Gable in Mogambo
- **1968** — YSL's Saharienne collection
- **1970s** — Banana Republic (original high-end safari-themed outfitter) revives the style
- **Present** — Bryceland's, The Armoury, Grenfell, Westley Richards, Private White V.C., Kaptain Sunshine, Fortela

## Key Design Features
- Four bellows pockets (two chest for quick access, two lower for bulkier items)
- Self-fabric belt (manages weight of loaded pockets)
- Broad collar (sun/wind protection, frames face)
- Cotton drill or poplin (breathable, thorn-resistant)
- Pale coloring (reflects sunlight, hides dust)
- Epaulettes (military origin, rank display)

## Hemingway's Bespoke Jacket (Willis & Geiger, 1936)
Specific features commissioned from field experience:
- Centre vent for movement
- Sleeve pocket sized for glasses
- Quilted recoil pad at shoulder
- Shotgun shell loops in lining
- Full bellows pockets upper and lower

Willis & Geiger also outfitted: Charles Lindbergh, Amelia Earhart, Edmund Hillary

## Contemporary Brands (from our sources + research)
- **Bryceland's** (Tokyo) — Solaro fabric, half belt, military back pleat. Made to order.
- **The Armoury** — bellows-and-gusset pockets that lie flat when empty, enlarged buttons, long-point collar
- **Grenfell** (England, since 1923) — heritage outerwear maker
- **Westley Richards** (Birmingham gunmaker) — Selous safari jacket for actual field use
- **Private White V.C.** (Manchester) — Lord's Jacket in heavyweight goatskin suede
- **Kaptain Sunshine** (Japan) — cotton/linen blend, P41-inspired
- **Fortela** (Italy) — Carlo safari jacket
- **Permanent Style / Budd** — bespoke safari in Irish linen, £395, solving the fit problem of off-the-rack versions

### Key insight from Permanent Style:
"The problem with safari jackets is that they often look rather colonial and middle-aged — partly the sand color and partly the fit. They rarely have waist suppression and tend to have large, blooming sleeves." Going bespoke (or choosing well-cut modern versions) solves this.

## Gaps
- Nobody has written about the tension between the jacket's colonial history and its modern appeal in a nuanced way
- The Hemingway bespoke details (glasses pocket, recoil pad) are rarely discussed — they show true equipment design thinking
- Japanese interpretations (Kaptain Sunshine, Bryceland's) treat it as craft rather than costume
