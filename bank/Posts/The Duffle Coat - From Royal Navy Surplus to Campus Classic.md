## Content

<p>The duffle coat takes its name from the Belgian town of Duffel, where the thick, napped woollen fabric was first produced. The British Royal Navy adopted the garment during World War II as cold-weather outerwear, choosing it for a specific reason: the toggle-and-loop fastening could be operated by sailors wearing thick gloves. Buttons were impractical on a frozen deck. Wooden toggles threaded through hemp rope loops were not.</p>
<p>The coat&#8217;s proportions were dictated by its military function: long enough to cover the thighs, roomy enough to fit over a uniform, and hooded to protect against wind and rain without requiring a separate hat. The patch pockets were large enough for charts and instruments.</p>
<p>Post-war, vast quantities of surplus duffle coats entered civilian markets at low prices. Students, in particular, adopted the coat as affordable, practical winter wear. The association with academic life — slightly shabby, intellectual, indifferent to fashion — became the coat&#8217;s defining identity for decades.</p>
<p>A well-made duffle coat in genuine Duffel cloth is remarkably warm. The thick, dense weave traps air efficiently, while the napped surface sheds light rain. The toggle closure, with its inherent looseness, allows ventilation when needed — something that zipped or buttoned coats handle less well. It&#8217;s a system that works precisely because it&#8217;s imprecise.</p>
