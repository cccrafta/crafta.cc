## Content

<p>Pick up a CPO shirt and the weight doesn't match the silhouette — placket, collar, two chest pockets, straight hem all read as shirting, but the fabric is dense melton, the same compressed wool a peacoat is built from, cut here to layer over a thermal and block wind off a ship deck. The Navy arrived at this combination because cold-weather deck duty needed a work shirt that could function as an outer layer: heavy enough to block wind, short enough to move in, without the weight or length of a uniform coat.</p>

<p>The U.S. Navy introduced the "Shirt, Wool, Blue" in the 1930s: single chest pocket with a scalloped flap, anchor-embossed buttons, no lining or insulation. A second pocket arrived in the late 1940s, and that dual-pocket version defined the garment civilians would come to know. Weight is the defining spec: naval melton runs approximately 20-26oz per square yard, well above shirting flannel at 8-10oz and just below peacoat cloth at 28oz. Below 18oz the CPO loses its wind resistance and structural density and becomes a heavy overshirt; above 28oz, it is coat territory. The melton reaches 20-26oz through milling: raw wool woven in twill, then fulled under moisture, heat, and friction until the fibres compress and partially felt, producing a surface that barely frays when cut and sheds water rather than absorbing it.</p>

<p>By the late 1960s the Navy had phased out 100% wool and the CPO left official issue, but it had been moving into civilian wardrobes for two decades already. After WWII, the American garment industry was producing roughly half its pre-war output; discharged sailors wore their issue clothing because civilian supply was short, not only for aesthetic reasons. Army-Navy surplus stores spread through the 1950s, selling peacoats, field jackets, and CPO shirts at prices civilian outerwear couldn't match. The pipeline moved through stages: veterans first, then beatniks and bikers, then countercultural students, until by 1968 GQ had declared "only hippies wear Army-Navy surplus." By 1972-74 it had crossed into mass-market teenage fashion at Sears and J.C. Penney, available in plaids and non-navy colorways for around $20. Each step widened the reach and diluted the specification: the mass-market versions preserved the silhouette but moved to lighter blends, because the 20oz melton was the expensive part. Al Pacino played Francis "Lion" Delbuchi in <em>Scarecrow</em> (1973) — a drifter, a former sailor — and costume designer Jo Ynocencio dressed him in a navy wool CPO: dark blue, long-pointed collar, still dressed for the deck while walking through the American midwest. Ynocencio chose it because Delbuchi had just come back from five years at sea.</p>

<p>Melton wool doesn't develop the kind of patina that builds a following. There is no fading, no crease memory that photographs, no dramatic surface change. What changes is tactile: after extended wear the dense surface softens at stress points — elbows, collar stand, the placket — and the garment starts moving with the body rather than against it. What emerges is not worn-looking but conformed, and the initial stiffness of quality melton means the heaviest versions require well over a hundred hours of wear before the fabric fully settles. The change is real but invisible to a camera, and a CPO worn for a decade looks much like one worn for a season, until you pick both up.</p>

<p>Buzz Rickson's, whose parent company Toyo Enterprise traces to Kosho & Co., a business founded in the 1940s making garments for American GIs stationed in Japan, reproduces the 1939 1st-model CPO in 100% melton wool on vintage looms, boiled and sun-dried, cotton thread throughout, chain-stitched seams, and the counter-rotated right cuff from the original specification; Real McCoy's works to the same standard. The institutional knowledge in both cases runs back to direct commercial contact with the era, not study of it. The American market today shows what happens when weight becomes secondary: Schott's current model runs 20oz in a 50/50 wool-polyester blend; Schott calls it "lightweight." Freenote Cloth's Midway CPO runs 10oz in a four-fibre blend — half the weight of the original range — with the silhouette intact and the outer-layer function gone. Cockpit USA's 2025 domestically made version at 20-22oz in 85/15 wool-nylon sits closer to original spec than most American brands have managed in decades. The spread from 10oz to 26oz across current production shows how far the name can travel from the thing it names.</p>

<p>Engineered Garments has been revisiting the CPO silhouette for nearly two decades — snap placket instead of buttons, asymmetrical chest pockets, zip pockets on the sleeves, curved hem — renegotiating the function with each season's fabric: heavy wool elastique, striped HB, corduroy. Iron Heart keeps the same construction standards (chainstitch runoff, felled seams, copper hardware) in 14.7oz selvedge denim, where the weather function is gone but the build quality isn't. Mister Freedom's FW2024 Bubblehead CPO, made in collaboration with Sugar Cane, holds the 1930s-40s tailoring exactly — fouled anchor buttons, scalloped cuffs, chin strap — in an 11oz cotton/sugarcane denim blend, the military specification running without the military material. Schott's 50/50 blend softens the spec to reach a price point; EG, Iron Heart, and Mister Freedom are treating the CPO as a design brief rather than a functional specification.</p>

<p>The CPO fills a gap that most wardrobes handle badly: the conditions between shirt weather and jacket weather. The garment that actually fills it needs the weight to do so. At 10oz it is a shirt with a CPO's pockets; at 26oz, a coat with a shirt's collar. The 20-26oz piece that reads as both works because the melton is dense enough to cut wind and hold its shape as an outer layer, while remaining light enough to sit under a coat when the temperature drops. What the original Navy specification understood, and what Buzz Rickson's and Real McCoy's still preserve, is that this identity is not carried by the anchor buttons or the scalloped pocket flaps, which are identifiers rather than the thing itself. The in-between quality that makes the garment functional in the gap it occupies is 20oz of milled wool — and the silhouette alone could never provide it.</p>
