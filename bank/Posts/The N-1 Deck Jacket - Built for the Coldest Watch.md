## Content

<p>The N-1 was issued to U.S. Navy personnel for deck duty in cold weather — standing watch on an aircraft carrier in the North Atlantic, where wind chill could drop temperatures well below freezing and spray would coat everything in ice. The jacket needed to keep a stationary person warm in the worst conditions the open ocean could produce.</p>
<p>The solution was a heavyweight cotton shell lined with alpaca pile — a deep, dense fur-like material that trapped an enormous amount of insulating air. The mouton fur collar could be raised and fastened to seal the neck completely. Knit cuffs and a drawstring hem prevented warm air from escaping.</p>
<p>The N-1&#8217;s proportions are distinctive: boxy through the torso to accommodate the thick lining, with slightly short sleeves that kept the cuffs out of the way during manual operations on deck. It&#8217;s not a flattering cut by contemporary standards, but it&#8217;s a purposeful one.</p>
<p>Original N-1 jackets from the 1940s remain functional today. The alpaca lining, if maintained, retains its insulating properties indefinitely. The cotton shell, treated with water-resistant finish, still repels moisture. Few modern jackets using synthetic insulation will perform as well after eighty years of existence.</p>
