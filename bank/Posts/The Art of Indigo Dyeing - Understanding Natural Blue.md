## Content

<p>Indigo is not a simple dye. Unlike most natural colorants that bond directly to fiber, indigo must be chemically reduced to a soluble form, applied to yarn, and then oxidized back to its insoluble state. This multi-step process is what gives indigo-dyed fabric its distinctive fading behavior — the pigment sits on the surface of fibers rather than penetrating them fully.</p>
<p>Traditional Japanese indigo dyeing — aizome — uses fermented indigo leaves in an alkaline solution. The vat is a living system, maintained like a sourdough starter, requiring daily attention to temperature, pH, and bacterial balance. Master dyers in Tokushima prefecture have maintained vats for decades, feeding them wheat bran and sake to keep the fermentation active.</p>
<p>The depth of color depends on repetition. A single dip produces a pale blue. Twelve or more dips yield a deep, almost black shade known as kachi-iro — historically associated with victory and strength. Each dip adds another thin layer of pigment, building up a color with physical depth that flat dyeing cannot achieve.</p>
<p>Synthetic indigo, developed in the late 19th century, is chemically identical to the natural compound. But the process of application matters as much as the molecule itself. Rope dyeing, the method used for premium denim, preserves the ring-dyeing effect that makes raw denim fade so distinctively — white core surrounded by indigo surface.</p>
