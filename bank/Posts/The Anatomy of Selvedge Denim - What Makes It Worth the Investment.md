## Content

<p>Selvedge denim gets its name from the &#8220;self-edge&#8221; — the tightly woven band running along the edge of the fabric that prevents unraveling. Unlike modern shuttle-less looms that produce wide bolts of fabric with cut, fraying edges, vintage shuttle looms create a narrow fabric with this clean, finished edge.</p>
<p>The width limitation of shuttle looms is precisely what makes selvedge special. At roughly 30 inches wide compared to modern denim&#8217;s 60+ inches, each yard takes longer to produce. The slower weaving process creates a denser, more irregular fabric with character that mass-produced denim simply cannot replicate.</p>
<p>Look at the outseam of any pair of selvedge jeans and you&#8217;ll find a folded seam exposing that distinctive edge — often with a colored thread line, typically red, running through it. This isn&#8217;t just aesthetic. It&#8217;s structural proof that the fabric was made on a shuttle loom, with all the density and durability that implies.</p>
<p>The real question isn&#8217;t whether selvedge is better — it&#8217;s whether you value the process as much as the product. A pair of raw selvedge jeans will mold to your body over months and years, developing a fade pattern that&#8217;s uniquely yours. That&#8217;s not a feature you can engineer into fast fashion.</p>
