## Content

<p>A hand-welted shoe begins with a wooden last carved to the exact dimensions of the client&#8217;s foot. The upper is cut from leather, stretched over the last, and lasted — pulled tight and tacked into place. Then the welt is stitched by hand through the upper and insole using a curved awl and waxed linen thread, one stitch at a time.</p>
<p>The hand-welting stitch is invisible from the outside. The shoemaker works from inside the shoe, piercing the insole at an angle to create a channel that conceals the stitching. Each stitch is locked individually, meaning that if one stitch breaks, the others remain secure. Machine welting uses a continuous chain stitch that can unravel if a single point fails.</p>
<p>After the welt is attached, the space between the insole and outsole is filled with cork or leather, creating a bed that will gradually compress and conform to the wearer&#8217;s foot. The outsole is then stitched to the welt — this outer row of stitching is the visible sole stitch you see on welted shoes.</p>
<p>The entire process takes between forty and sixty hours of skilled labor per pair. This investment of time produces a shoe that can be rebuilt almost indefinitely — the upper, if well-maintained, will outlast multiple rounds of resoling. A bespoke hand-welted shoe is not bought for a season. It&#8217;s bought for a lifetime.</p>
