## Content

<p>The M-65 field jacket. The MA-1 bomber. The peacoat. The cargo pant. The henley. Nearly every foundational piece in the modern casual wardrobe originated as military issue. This isn&#8217;t coincidence — it&#8217;s the natural result of governments spending enormous resources to design functional, durable clothing at scale.</p>
<p>Military garments are designed by committee to solve specific problems: keep soldiers warm, dry, mobile, and able to carry equipment. The design process is ruthlessly practical. If a feature doesn&#8217;t serve a function, it&#8217;s eliminated. If a construction method isn&#8217;t durable enough, it&#8217;s reinforced. This utilitarian design philosophy produces garments with an honesty that fashion rarely achieves on its own.</p>
<p>The post-war surplus boom of the 1940s and 50s flooded civilian markets with well-made, affordable clothing. Workers, students, and counterculture figures adopted military garments for practical and symbolic reasons. By the time fashion designers started referencing military style in the 1960s and 70s, the aesthetic was already deeply embedded in street-level dressing.</p>
<p>Today, the influence is so pervasive it&#8217;s almost invisible. Olive drab, khaki, and navy blue — all military colors — form the backbone of most men&#8217;s wardrobes. The field jacket silhouette appears in everything from luxury fashion to fast fashion. We&#8217;re all wearing uniforms; we just get to choose which ones.</p>
