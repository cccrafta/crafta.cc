## Content

<p>In 1865, the Prince of Wales asked his tailor at Henry Poole and Co. on Savile Row for something that did not yet exist: an evening jacket without tails. What he received was a short, ventless coat in midnight blue silk, cut with formal faced lapels but freed from the tailcoat's rigid skirts. It was intended for private dinners at Sandringham, where the prince wanted to eat comfortably without the ceremony of full evening dress. Simon Cundey, Henry Poole's current managing director, puts it simply: Edward VII and Henry Poole designed the dinner suit between them. The garment crossed the Atlantic in 1886 when James Brown Potter, an American member of New York's Tuxedo Club, wore a version made for him by the same house. The club gave the jacket its American name. Britain kept calling it a dinner jacket. France, remembering its ancestry, called it le smoking.</p>

<p>The fabric that makes a dinner jacket work is not incidental to its design — it is the design. Barathea, a satin-group weave of worsted wool, became the British standard because of its depth of colour and clean drape. The weight sits between 9–10oz — light enough for hours of wear in heated rooms, structured enough to hold its shape through an evening of standing, sitting, and dining. Mohair blended at 14–16% adds crispness and lustre without weight. The body cloth must be matte, because the silk-faced lapels already provide sheen — satin for a smooth lustre, grosgrain for a subtler ribbed texture that British tailors associate with custom work. The contrast between matte body and lustrous lapel is not decoration. It is what distinguishes evening wear from a dark suit under candlelight. Midnight blue, which the Prince of Wales championed in the 1920s, reads as true black under artificial light, while actual black tends toward brown. The colour choice is optical engineering, not preference.</p>

<p>The internal construction solves problems that the wearer never sees. A full body canvas — horsehair at the chest, domette layered over it — runs the length of the jacket, giving the front its drape without stiffness. Two darts on each side cut the waist in cleanly. The lapels are not simply folded fabric: they are faced in silk — satin or grosgrain — and hand-attached on the best examples, so the roll sits naturally rather than being pressed into shape. The trouser waistband sits higher than on a day suit, because the wearer will spend hours seated and a lower rise would bind at the hip. There are no belt loops; side adjusters handle fit invisibly, since a cummerbund or waistcoat covers the waist entirely. The back is ventless or double-vented — never a single centre vent, which opens when reaching into pockets and exposes the seat. Every decision in the construction assumes a specific scenario: a long evening, a heated room, candlelight, and a body that needs to move between standing, sitting, and dining without the jacket losing its composure.</p>

<p>The tuxedo reached its peak in the 1930s, when Hollywood made formal dressing visible to millions. Fred Astaire's tailcoat in Top Hat, made by Kilgour French and Stanbury in 1935, was engineered as much as it was tailored — an enlarged sleevehead and exaggerated rope at the shoulder gave his arms room to move through full choreography, while the waistcoat edges were rounded rather than pointed so they would not catch on a raised leg. At Anderson and Sheppard, Astaire would dance up and down the fitting hall to test that his collar stayed anchored and nothing pulled in the crotch. He once said he would throw a brand-new suit against the wall a few times to get the stiff, square newness out of it. The 1930s also democratised the garment: S. Rudofker's Sons in Philadelphia mass-produced ready-to-wear tuxedos, tuxedo rental became available for the first time, and the American high-school prom — descended from debutante balls — turned the dinner jacket into a rite of passage rather than a class marker.</p>

<p>By 1964, the Academy Awards had extended its red carpet and begun broadcasting the arrivals, turning the tuxedo into a global spectacle viewed in over 200 countries. But the garment's context was already narrowing. Formal clothing had been mothballed during the Second World War, and when it returned, black tie shifted from standard evening wear to special-occasion dress. The 1960s counterculture targeted the suit as a symbol of conformity. Silicon Valley, founded by children of that counterculture, eliminated the tie from professional life by 1990. Peter Thiel wrote in 2014 that you should never invest in a tech CEO who wears a suit. The tuxedo did not disappear; it simply became reserved for the occasions that still demanded it: galas, premieres, ceremonies.</p>

<p>The garment adapted by loosening its own construction. Each generation removed structure: padded shoulders gave way to softer, natural lines; full canvas was replaced in many ready-to-wear versions by half-canvas or fused fronts; the waistcoat disappeared, then the cummerbund, then the shirt studs, then the bow tie itself. In 1966, Yves Saint Laurent's Le Smoking showed that the architecture could be rebuilt entirely for a different body. He narrowed the jacket's waistline, adjusted the trouser rise to sit on a wider hip, softened the collar curve, and cut the shoulders closer. The construction changes were specific and deliberate — not a men's jacket in a smaller size, but a rethinking of where the garment needed structure and where it needed to give. The result proved something about the tuxedo that decades of incremental relaxation had only hinted at: the underlying architecture — lapel facings, waist definition, trouser stripe, the contrast of matte body and lustrous detail — could be separated from any particular cut and still function as evening wear.</p>

<p>The tuxedo's influence now extends well beyond the garment itself. The silk-faced lapel migrated onto blazers and casual jackets — a single tuxedo detail applied to a garment that has nothing to do with formal evening wear. Grosgrain-trimmed trousers appear paired with knitwear and sneakers, stripped of their original context but retaining the visual sharpness that the trouser stripe was designed to create. The collarless dinner jacket, which houses like Dior and Celine have produced in recent seasons, removes the most traditional element while keeping the silhouette. The tuxedo's DNA has dispersed into what might be called smart evening — garments that borrow individual details rather than adopting the full ensemble. Whether this dilutes or extends the garment's legacy depends on whether the tuxedo is a fixed object or a set of principles — and the construction details suggest it has always been the latter.</p>

<p>Henry Poole still produces dinner jackets on Savile Row. Cifonelli in Paris cuts them with a roped shoulder invented by Lorenzo Cifonelli's grandfather. Anderson and Sheppard still uses the soft drape cut that Astaire danced in. The construction details that Edward VII's tailor established in 1865 survive not because they are traditional but because the environment they were designed for has not changed. A formal evening still involves hours of standing, sitting, dining, and being seen under warm artificial light. The garment that solves those problems today is, in its essentials, the same garment that solved them in 1865. A century and a half of loosening has altered what the wearer puts on — but not what the garment needs to do.</p>
