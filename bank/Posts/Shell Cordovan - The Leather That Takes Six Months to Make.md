## Content

<p>Shell cordovan is not tanned from the outer skin of an animal. It comes from a dense, fibrous membrane found beneath the hide on a horse&#8217;s hindquarters — the &#8220;shell.&#8221; This membrane has no pores, no grain pattern, and a structure unlike any other leather. Extracting it requires skilled hand work, shaving away layers of flesh and hide to reveal the shell beneath.</p>
<p>The tanning process is vegetable-based and takes approximately six months. The shells are slowly treated in pits of tree bark solution, turned and monitored throughout. After tanning, they&#8217;re stuffed with grease, dried, and hand-glazed with a glass tool to bring up the characteristic deep shine.</p>
<p>The resulting leather has properties that cowhide cannot match. Shell cordovan doesn&#8217;t crease — it rolls. Flex a shoe made from shell and you&#8217;ll see smooth, undulating waves rather than the sharp lines that form in calfskin. This rolling quality means the leather maintains its shape far longer than conventional materials.</p>
<p>Production is limited by biology. Each horse yields only two small shells, and the supply of raw material depends on the horse meat industry in Europe and Japan. A single tannery may process only a few thousand shells per year, which is why shell cordovan commands prices ten times higher than premium calfskin.</p>
