## Content

<p>In 1858, the French Navy codified the specifications of its standard-issue marinière: twenty-one white stripes across the body, each twice the width of the fourteen to fifteen blue stripes between them. The number of white stripes supposedly corresponded to Napoleon&#8217;s victories, though this origin story is likely apocryphal.</p>
<p>The practical purpose of the striped shirt was visibility. A sailor who fell overboard wearing a striped shirt was easier to spot against the dark water than one wearing a solid color. The tight-knit cotton jersey provided warmth and dried quickly — essential qualities for life at sea.</p>
<p>The Breton stripe entered civilian fashion through the coastal towns of Brittany, where the marinière was everyday wear for fishermen and dockworkers. Artists working in these towns adopted it as practical studio wear. Through art and photography, the striped shirt became associated with a particular kind of creative, casual elegance.</p>
<p>The stripe pattern works because of its visual simplicity. Horizontal stripes on a fitted body create a sense of structure without the formality of vertical lines. The pattern is strong enough to stand as a statement but neutral enough to combine with almost anything. It is one of the few garment patterns that has never gone in or out of fashion — it has simply persisted.</p>
