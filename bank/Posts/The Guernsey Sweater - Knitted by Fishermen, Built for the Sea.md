## Content

<p>The Guernsey — also called a gansey — was knitted by the wives and mothers of fishermen in the Channel Islands, using tightly twisted five-ply wool that retained its lanolin content. The natural oils made the sweater nearly waterproof, capable of shedding spray and light rain without absorbing moisture.</p>
<p>Construction followed strict functional logic. The body was knitted in the round — no side seams — to eliminate weak points where water could penetrate. Underarm gussets allowed free movement for hauling nets and ropes. The neckline sat high and tight to seal out wind.</p>
<p>Each port developed distinctive stitch patterns that served as a form of identification. The patterns weren&#8217;t decorative choices — they were practical markers. If a fisherman was lost at sea, the pattern on his sweater could identify his home village.</p>
<p>A traditionally knitted Guernsey is virtually indestructible. The tight gauge and worsted-spun yarn create a fabric so dense it resists abrasion, wind, and water simultaneously. Modern outdoor gear achieves similar performance only through layering multiple synthetic materials — the Guernsey does it with a single piece of knitted wool.</p>
