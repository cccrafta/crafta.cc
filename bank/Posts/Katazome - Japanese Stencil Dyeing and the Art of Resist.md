## Content

<p>Katazome is a Japanese resist-dyeing technique that uses a paste made from rice bran and rice flour, applied through hand-cut paper stencils, to create patterns on fabric. Where the paste is applied, the dye cannot penetrate. Where the fabric is left exposed, it absorbs color. The result is precise, repeatable patterns with the warmth and irregularity of handwork.</p>
<p>The stencils themselves are works of craft. Cut from layers of mulberry paper laminated with persimmon tannin, they must be detailed enough to create complex patterns yet strong enough to survive repeated application of sticky rice paste. Master stencil cutters spend years learning to control the small, sharp blades used to pierce the paper.</p>
<p>The rice paste is applied with a wooden spatula, forced through the stencil openings onto cloth stretched on a long board. The stencil is then carefully lifted and repositioned for the next repeat. A single bolt of fabric might require dozens of applications, each aligned precisely with the previous one. Any error in registration creates a visible flaw in the final pattern.</p>
<p>After the paste dries, the fabric is dyed — usually with indigo — then washed to remove the paste and reveal the pattern. The edges of katazome patterns have a quality that printed patterns lack: a slight softness where the paste didn&#8217;t seal perfectly, where dye crept just slightly under the resist. This quality is not a mistake. It&#8217;s what makes the technique human.</p>
