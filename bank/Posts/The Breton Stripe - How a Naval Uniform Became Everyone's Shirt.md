## Content

<p>Twenty-one white stripes on a tubular-knit body, no side seams, the cotton dense enough to hold wind and shed spray. When the French parliament passed the Act of France in 1858, codifying the marinière — named for the sailors of Brittany, the northwestern peninsula where the Navy was based — as naval uniform, every specification had a practical origin. The tubular knit meant nothing could snag on rigging. The absence of side seams made laundering simple — a shirt that could be wrung out and dried flat on deck without warping. The stripes themselves served visibility: a sailor overboard in a striped shirt showed against dark water where a solid colour disappeared. The twenty-one white stripes are sometimes attributed to Napoleon's victories, though the claim is almost certainly apocryphal. What is certain is that every detail of the garment was designed for function, not decoration. It was, in the purest sense, workwear.</p>

<p>The Breton did not cross into civilian life through marketing or endorsement. It migrated through proximity. Fishermen in Brittany wore it because it was available; artists in coastal towns wore it because fishermen did. By the time Coco Chanel opened her Deauville boutique in 1913, the marinière was already embedded in the visual culture of the French coast. Chanel didn't discover it — she recognised what it already communicated: ease without carelessness, structure without rigidity. She cut it in jersey, paired it with wide-legged trousers, and made it legible to a different audience. Through the 1920s, the Breton became shorthand for French style. Not because Chanel invented the association, but because the shirt's proportions — the wide boat neck that opened the collarbone, the horizontal lines that broadened the silhouette, the heavy jersey that held its shape without stiffness — already solved the problem she was trying to solve: how to look composed without looking constructed.</p>

<p>Then the pattern began attracting people who had no connection to the sea at all. Picasso wore it almost daily in his studio at Vallauris — there are more photographs of him in a marinière than without one. James Dean wore it in New York in the 1950s. Jean Seberg wore it in Breathless. Andy Warhol wore it at the Factory. Brigitte Bardot wore it in Saint-Tropez. None of these people coordinated. None were referencing each other. The connection was not mystical — it was practical. The marinière was cheap, durable, and bold enough to look deliberate without requiring thought. For people whose attention was directed at their work rather than their clothing, that combination was difficult to improve upon. Picasso confirmed as much in 1944 when a journalist asked why he painted himself as a sailor; he opened his shirt to reveal striped underwear beneath and said, simply, "because I always wear a sailor's shirt." It was not a style choice. It was the absence of one — and that absence, photographed often enough, became a style of its own.</p>

<p>Jean Paul Gaultier, who wore a marinière to his first men's show in 1978, was the first to ask why it worked rather than simply wearing it. From 1983 onward he made the stripe his house signature — but his contribution was separating the pattern from the garment. He printed the stripe onto corsets, mesh tops, tailored suits, perfume bottles. In doing so, he identified what the Navy had understood in 1858 and Picasso had felt instinctively: horizontal lines create immediate recognition, structured rhythm, and visual presence that works at any distance and on any surface.</p>

<p>The mills that produce them have histories nearly as long as the garment itself. Saint James has been knitting in Normandy since 1889. Armor Lux has operated from Quimper since 1938. Both still produce marinières on the same principles: heavy cotton, tubular construction, the original stripe proportions. The Breton is one of the few garments where the heritage manufacturers remain the best source. The pattern has been copied endlessly, but the construction — that dense, seamless, tubular knit — is what separates a marinière from a striped shirt.</p>

<p>Most garments that endure do so because they adapt. The Breton endures because it hasn't needed to. The same shirt worn by French sailors in the 1860s is, in its essentials, the same shirt produced by Saint James today. What changed was not the garment but who reached for it. The pattern didn't follow culture — it sat still, and culture kept arriving. That is the rarest quality a garment can possess: the capacity to mean something different to every person who wears it, while remaining exactly what it has always been.</p>
