## Content

<p>The safari jacket was engineered for a specific environment: the African bush, where temperatures swing from scorching daytime heat to cold nights, where you need to carry maps, ammunition, and tools on your person, and where laundry service doesn&#8217;t exist. The design response was a belted cotton jacket with four bellows pockets, a self-fabric belt, and a cut loose enough to allow air circulation.</p>
<p>The four-pocket layout wasn&#8217;t arbitrary. Each pocket served a purpose: two chest pockets with button flaps for frequently accessed items, two lower pockets with pleated bellows for bulkier gear. The belt cinched the waist to prevent the loaded pockets from pulling the jacket out of shape.</p>
<p>The fabric choice — typically a tight-weave cotton drill or poplin — balanced durability with breathability. Light enough to wear in equatorial heat, tough enough to resist thorns and brush. The pale color reflected sunlight and showed dirt less than darker alternatives.</p>
<p>The safari jacket entered mainstream fashion through photography and film, where it became shorthand for adventurous sophistication. Designers refined the proportions — slimmer body, narrower pockets, lighter fabrics — but the essential architecture remains recognizable. Four pockets, a belt, a collar: the same problem-solving design, adapted for different problems.</p>
