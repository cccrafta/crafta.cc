## Content

<p>When archaeologists unwrapped Egyptian mummies dating to 5000 BCE, they found linen — but they also found ramie, a bast fiber pulled from the stalks of Boehmeria nivea, intact after seven millennia in the tomb. Ramie's tensile strength exceeds cotton by roughly 600 percent, outperforms linen and wool, and rivals silk in luster. The fiber absorbs moisture rapidly and releases it just as fast, making it one of the most efficient natural textiles for warm climates. Yet walk into any contemporary fabric store, and ramie is almost nowhere to be found.</p>
<p>For most of recorded textile history, ramie held an important place. In China, where it has been cultivated for at least three thousand years, it was known as "China grass" and woven into everything from fishing nets to ceremonial garments. Japanese artisans used ramie — called karamushi — to produce jofu, a fine summer cloth from the Echigo region (present-day Niigata Prefecture) that earned UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage recognition in 2009. Across Southeast Asia, from the Philippines to Indonesia, ramie cloths served both practical and ritual functions. The fiber's natural resistance to bacteria and mildew made it a logical choice in humid equatorial climates long before anyone had a word for "antimicrobial."</p>
<p>Ramie's decline in the modern era comes down to processing, not performance. The raw fiber clings to its woody bark with a gummy pectin layer that must be removed through labor-intensive degumming — traditionally involving soaking, scraping, and chemical treatment. Even after extraction, the fiber is stiff and brittle until it has been washed repeatedly, a characteristic that frustrated industrial-scale manufacturers accustomed to cotton's cooperative behavior on spinning machinery. The economics tilted further against ramie when synthetic fibers arrived in the mid-twentieth century, offering wrinkle resistance and low cost that ramie could not match on a price-per-yard basis.</p>
<p>What makes ramie worth reconsidering now is precisely what made it inconvenient before: it gets softer and stronger with every wash, developing a drape that cotton loses over time. A ramie shirt after fifty washes outperforms a cotton shirt after five. The fiber is naturally breathable, dries faster than linen, and holds dye with minimal fading. For summer garments — unstructured blazers, camp-collar shirts, wide-leg trousers — ramie offers a combination of coolness and durability that few natural fibers can equal. Small-batch producers in Japan and South Korea have already begun reintroducing it into warm-weather collections, often blended with cotton or silk to offset the initial stiffness.</p>
<p>The fashion industry talks constantly about rediscovering forgotten materials, but most of that conversation circles back to the same handful of heritage fabrics. Ramie sits outside that rotation — too obscure for trend cycles, too demanding for fast production, and too good to ignore forever. A fiber that outlasted Egyptian tombs and still improves after decades of use does not need marketing. It needs a manufacturer willing to be patient with it.</p>
