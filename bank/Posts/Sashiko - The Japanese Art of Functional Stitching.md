## Content

<p>Sashiko began as necessity. In northern Japan, where cotton was scarce and winters were brutal, rural workers reinforced their clothing with rows of small running stitches. The technique served multiple purposes: it strengthened thin fabric, it added insulation by layering cloth together, and it extended the life of garments that couldn&#8217;t easily be replaced.</p>
<p>The stitching patterns that emerged weren&#8217;t arbitrary. Geometric designs like the diamond-shaped &#8220;persimmon flower&#8221; or the interlocking &#8220;seven treasures&#8221; created even distribution of reinforcement across the fabric. The patterns also carried meaning — certain motifs were believed to bring good fortune or ward off evil.</p>
<p>The technique requires minimal tools: a long needle, thick cotton thread, and a thimble worn on the palm rather than the fingertip. The stitch itself is simple — a basic running stitch — but achieving even spacing and consistent tension across complex patterns demands practice and patience.</p>
<p>Modern makers have embraced sashiko for visible mending, using it to repair jeans, jackets, and bags with decorative stitching that celebrates rather than conceals the damage. A blown-out knee patched with sashiko becomes a feature, not a flaw. The repair adds value rather than diminishing it.</p>
