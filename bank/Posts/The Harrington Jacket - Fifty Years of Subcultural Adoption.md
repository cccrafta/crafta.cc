## Content

<p>The Harrington is a lightweight blouson jacket with a stand collar, elasticated hem and cuffs, raglan sleeves, and a distinctive tartan lining. It was originally produced as a golf jacket — the raglan sleeves and short body allowed a full swing without restriction. The zip front and stand collar provided just enough protection from light wind and rain.</p>
<p>The jacket&#8217;s adoption by British youth subcultures in the 1960s transformed it from sportswear to streetwear. Mods wore it. Skinheads wore it. Punks wore it. Each group adopted the same basic garment and made it mean something different through context — what you wore it with, how you wore it, and what music you listened to while wearing it.</p>
<p>The Harrington&#8217;s genius is its neutrality. The silhouette is clean enough to serve as a blank canvas for whatever identity the wearer projects onto it. It doesn&#8217;t signify wealth, rebellion, or conformity on its own. It signifies whatever the wearer brings to it, which is why it has survived every fashion cycle since the mid-twentieth century.</p>
<p>The construction is straightforward: a cotton or polyester-cotton blend shell, a check lining, minimal hardware. There is nothing about a Harrington that justifies a premium price except the quality of the materials and the consistency of the cut. This simplicity is its strength — and the reason knockoffs rarely get it right.</p>
