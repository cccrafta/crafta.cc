## Content

<p>Cashmere is a fiber, not a weave. It can be knitted or woven into any construction, but whatever the construction, the fiber's properties dominate. At 14–19 microns in diameter — a human hair runs to 75 or more — each strand contains a hollow air chamber that traps still air against the body. A 200g cashmere sweater provides the same insulation as a 600g wool jumper: three times the warmth at a third of the mass. The trade-off is structural. Wool's high-frequency crimp gives it spring and resilience; cashmere's irregular crimp produces drape and loft but no architectural rigidity. A cashmere sweater follows the body. A cashmere overcoat settles rather than holds a silhouette. The fiber excels wherever warmth matters more than shape, and it fails wherever a garment needs to stand on its own.</p>

<p>Hold cashmere and wool side by side and the difference registers at the surface before the weight. Wool pushes back against the thumb; cashmere gives. The microscopic scales on each cashmere fiber are fewer and flatter than wool's, which is why cashmere does not itch against skin and why it takes dye differently: the fiber's nearly round cross-section absorbs colour evenly, producing a depth in mid-tones — oatmeal, tobacco, slate, navy — that coarser fibers scatter into brightness. The finishing that produces cashmere's characteristic softness is itself a craft: traditionally, dried teasel pods are used to raise the nap, their naturally hooked bracts drawing loose fibers to the surface. Unlike metal-tine machines, a teasel hook breaks before it damages the fiber, making it self-limiting — a technique the Romans used on cloth and that the finest mills still prefer for cashmere today. The nap is raised, shorn close, raised again, the cloth kept damp throughout. What reaches the customer is the result of a process that treats the fiber as something that can be damaged by the tools designed to improve it.</p>

<p>One goat produces 150–200 grams of this fiber per year. A sweater takes three to four animals; an overcoat, thirty to forty. The undercoat is hand-combed during a few weeks of spring shedding — combed, not shorn, because combing yields a higher ratio of fine fiber to coarse guard hair. Global annual production stands at roughly 6,500 metric tons of pure cashmere against 2 million metric tons of sheep's wool. This scarcity is ancient. Cashmere has been woven in Kashmir since the 3rd century BCE; the finest pashmina shawls were handloomed over months and reserved for Mughal aristocracy. When Napoleon's officers brought Kashmir shawls to Paris after the Egyptian campaign of 1798, Empress Joséphine dismissed them as "hideous" — then accumulated three to four hundred. Her public appearances wearing them generated a demand that European mills in Norwich, Edinburgh, and Paisley attempted to meet with imitations. The gap between the original and the copy has never fully closed.</p>

<p>Industrial cashmere required Joseph Dawson's mechanical de-hairing machine, invented in the 1870s by a Yorkshire mill-worker after an overland journey through India. His invention made Hawick, in the Scottish Borders, the center of global cashmere knitwear. William Lockie, founded there in 1874, still pays $135 per kilogram for Inner Mongolian cashmere — sourced at auction in Canton from herders whose goats, due to a combination of terrain, diet, and climate, produce the finest commercial fiber available. Lockie knits at tighter tension than most competitors, which uses more raw material per garment but creates a denser fabric that holds its shape over years. Each sweater is constructed from nine separately knitted pieces, hand-linked point by point — a process that Italian production, optimised for softness and speed, typically skips. Johnstons of Elgin, founded 1797, controls the full process from raw fiber to finished garment. Barrie Knitwear, founded 1903 and nearly shuttered before Chanel acquired it in 2012, judges the washing of each garment individually and runs its own training school because the finishing techniques cannot be learned elsewhere.</p>

<p>Cashmere pills. Every cashmere garment pills. The short, fine fibers tangle at friction points — under the arm, at cuffs, where a bag strap crosses the shoulder — and form small knots of loose fiber at the surface. This is not failure; it is the settling-in period that separates cashmere from synthetics. Quality cashmere pills early and then stabilizes as the loose surface fibers work out, leaving a fabric smoother and more coherent than when new. Cheap cashmere, knitted loosely and finished with chemical softeners that wash away after a season, continues thinning until the garment is unwearable. The indicators are fiber length (longer fibers pill less because they hold in the yarn), knit density (tight knitting traps fibers; loose knitting releases them), and ply count: two-ply is the minimum for durability; four-ply, which Permanent Style described as "a quality no one really sells anymore," produces garments that last decades. A cashmere cardigan documented by the same publication — knitted in 2009 — was sent after fifteen years for washing, de-pilling, and repair at £87. It then looked new.</p>

<p>What the heritage mills established — that cashmere rewards tight construction and patient finishing — newer makers have extended in directions the mills never intended. Brunello Cucinelli built an Umbrian enterprise around cashmere's capacity for muted, deep colour and slow, humanistic production. Extreme Cashmere in Amsterdam makes over 100 styles in one-size, genderless cuts with fluorescent colourways, ships a cashmere comb with every purchase, and installs washing stations in its flagship — treating care as part of the product. In Tokyo, Ryota Iwai's Auralee develops 75% of its textiles as proprietary Japanese fabrications, with Iwai travelling personally to Mongolia to source fiber and letting the material dictate the silhouette. In Los Angeles, The Elder Statesman hand-spins cashmere and tie-dyes it into patterns no Scottish mill would recognise. The fiber carries all of them — heritage precision, quiet luxury, genderless experiment, counterculture craft — because the properties that make cashmere valuable are physical, not cultural: hollow chambers, fine diameter, hygroscopic response. The goat produces 150 grams a year regardless of what the maker intends to do with it, and the maker who understands this treats the fiber not as a luxury input but as a constraint that demands the design be worth the material.</p>
