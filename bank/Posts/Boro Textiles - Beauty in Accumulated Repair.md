## Content

<p>Boro is a Japanese term meaning &#8220;tattered&#8221; or &#8220;repaired.&#8221; It describes textiles — typically indigo-dyed cotton — that have been patched and mended so extensively that the original fabric is barely visible beneath layers of accumulated repair. What remains is a dense, complex surface of overlapping patches held together by sashiko stitching.</p>
<p>These textiles originated from poverty. In rural northern Japan, cotton was expensive and had to be imported from warmer regions. Families maintained their textiles for generations, adding patches as holes appeared, stitching new layers over worn areas, repurposing old garments as patches for newer ones. A single boro futon cover might contain fabric spanning a hundred years of family use.</p>
<p>The aesthetic that emerges from this process is accidental but powerful. The varying shades of indigo — some patches faded nearly white, others still deep blue — create a visual depth that no intentional design process would produce. The stitching patterns vary as different hands added repairs over decades. The whole is greater than the sum of its patches.</p>
<p>Contemporary appreciation of boro raises uncomfortable questions about aestheticizing poverty. The original makers didn&#8217;t intend to create art — they were keeping warm. But the principles embedded in boro practice — that repair is preferable to replacement, that utility and beauty coexist, that objects improve through use — resonate with how many people want to relate to their possessions today.</p>
