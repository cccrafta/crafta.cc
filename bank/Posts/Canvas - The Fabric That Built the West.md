## Content

<p>Canvas is a plain-weave fabric made from tightly twisted cotton, linen, or hemp yarns. The word comes from the Latin &#8220;cannabis&#8221; — hemp — which was the original fiber used in sailcloth, the first major application of canvas. The same material that propelled ships across oceans was eventually cut and sewn into tents, bags, and clothing for the people who settled the territories those ships reached.</p>
<p>The properties of canvas are straightforward: it is stiff, it is strong, and it is abrasion-resistant. A heavy cotton canvas — 12 ounces per square yard or more — can withstand dragging across rock, repeated folding under load, and decades of hard use. It does this through material honesty rather than engineering: the weave is simply very tight and the yarn is simply very strong.</p>
<p>Waxed canvas adds water resistance without sacrificing breathability. The wax fills the spaces between yarns, preventing water from penetrating while still allowing air and moisture vapor to pass through. Unlike coated synthetics, waxed canvas develops character with use — the wax cracks and wears at fold points, creating a patina unique to each piece.</p>
<p>Canvas lacks the technical performance of modern synthetic fabrics. It&#8217;s heavier, it dries slower, and it offers no stretch. What it offers instead is longevity measured in generations rather than seasons, a material quality that improves with age, and a connection to a manufacturing tradition that predates industrialization.</p>
