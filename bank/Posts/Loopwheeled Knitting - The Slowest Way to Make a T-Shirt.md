## Content

<p>A modern circular knitting machine produces jersey fabric at roughly 30 revolutions per minute. A loopwheel machine — a pre-war design — turns at about one revolution per minute. This thirty-fold difference in speed is the entire argument for loopwheeled knitwear.</p>
<p>At low speed, the machine places each loop of yarn with minimal tension. The resulting fabric has a loft and softness that high-speed knitting crushes out of the material. Pick up a loopwheeled sweatshirt and a conventional one of the same weight, and the difference is immediately apparent in hand feel.</p>
<p>Only a handful of manufacturers still operate loopwheel machines, mostly in Wakayama, Japan. The machines themselves are irreplaceable — no one makes them anymore, and each one requires constant maintenance and skilled operation. A single machine produces enough fabric for roughly 10-12 garments per day.</p>
<p>The practical advantage is longevity. Loopwheeled fabric holds its shape better because the low-tension knitting allows the cotton to maintain its natural elasticity. A loopwheeled sweatshirt worn for years will still spring back to its original dimensions after washing, while a conventional equivalent stretches and sags.</p>
<p>Whether this justifies a price tag five or ten times higher than a standard sweatshirt depends on what you value. If clothing is disposable, loopwheeled fabric makes no sense. If you want a single sweatshirt that improves with age, there&#8217;s nothing better.</p>
