## Content

<p>Corduroy is a ridged fabric created by weaving extra sets of yarn into a base cloth, then cutting the floats to create rows of soft tufted pile. The width of these ridges — called wales — determines the fabric&#8217;s character. Wide-wale corduroy with thick ridges reads as casual and rugged. Pinwale cord, with its fine, barely visible ridges, approaches the refinement of velvet.</p>
<p>The fabric&#8217;s origins are debated, but its name likely derives from the French &#8220;corde du roi&#8221; — though this etymology is itself disputed. What&#8217;s certain is that by the 18th century, corduroy was established as a working-class fabric in England, valued for its durability and warmth.</p>
<p>Corduroy&#8217;s great advantage is its ability to shift register. The same fabric that works as a farmer&#8217;s trouser also works as a professor&#8217;s jacket. Cut it slim and it reads urban. Cut it wide and it reads rural. Dye it olive or brown and it&#8217;s workwear. Dye it burgundy or mustard and it&#8217;s fashion. Few fabrics are this versatile.</p>
<p>The pile surface ages beautifully, wearing smooth at points of contact — the knees, the elbows, the seat — creating a gradual pattern of use that mirrors the way denim fades. A well-worn pair of corduroy trousers develops a sheen at the knee and a softness everywhere else that no amount of processing can replicate.</p>
