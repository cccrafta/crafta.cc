## Content

<p>The French chore coat — the veste de travail — was never designed to be fashionable. It was designed to be worn by painters, farmers, and factory workers who needed a lightweight outer layer with enough pockets to hold tools. The boxy cut allowed free movement. The cotton twill or moleskin fabric was tough enough for manual labor but light enough for warm weather.</p>
<p>What made the chore coat cross over from workwear to fashion was its perfect simplicity. Three patch pockets, a button front, a collar that sits flat — there&#8217;s nothing extraneous. Every element serves a function, and that functional honesty is exactly what gives it its appeal.</p>
<p>The standard blue — bleu de travail — wasn&#8217;t chosen for aesthetic reasons. It was simply the cheapest dye available in 19th-century France. Indigo was abundant and the color hid stains well. That utilitarian choice became an iconic design element that still defines the garment today.</p>
<p>Modern interpretations range from faithful reproductions in deadstock French fabric to designer versions in Japanese selvedge. But the best chore coats still follow the original proportions: slightly cropped, shoulders that sit naturally, and a width that allows layering without bulk.</p>
