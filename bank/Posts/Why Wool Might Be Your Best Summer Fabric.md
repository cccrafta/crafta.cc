## Content

<p>Hold a swatch of seven-ounce tropical wool up to a window and light pours through. The weave is so open, so deliberately porous, that it resembles gauze more than anything associated with winter overcoats. This is fresco cloth — a fabric engineered in early twentieth-century Britain specifically for colonial officers stationed in equatorial heat — and its performance in warm weather embarrasses cotton.</p>
<p>The distinction comes down to fiber behavior at the molecular level. Wool fibers are covered in overlapping scales that create tiny channels, wicking perspiration away from the skin and delivering it to the fabric&#8217;s outer surface, where it evaporates. Cotton, by contrast, absorbs moisture into its core and holds it there, which is why a cotton shirt on a humid August afternoon eventually feels like a wet towel pressed against the chest. Merino wool can absorb up to thirty percent of its weight in moisture before it even begins to feel damp. Cotton reaches that clammy threshold at around seven percent.</p>
<p>Fresco cloth amplifies this natural advantage through construction. The yarn is twisted far more tightly than in conventional worsteds — sometimes exceeding two hundred turns per meter — which makes the individual threads harder and springier. When woven into an open plain weave at seven to nine ounces per yard, the resulting fabric holds its shape away from the body, creating an air gap that functions as a cooling system. Winter-weight wools, by comparison, run twelve to fourteen ounces in dense twill weaves designed to trap warmth. The difference is architectural as much as material.</p>
<p>The history reinforces the science. When London tailors at firms like Hardy Amies and H. Huntsman dressed diplomats and military officers for postings in Singapore, Aden, and Lagos during the 1930s and 1940s, they reached for high-twist wool, not linen or cotton. The cloth survived tropical humidity without wilting, resisted wrinkles through long days in stifling offices, and dried quickly after monsoon downpours. That empirical track record across decades of equatorial service is difficult to argue with.</p>
<p>Perhaps the most useful test for any summer fabric has nothing to do with fiber content at all. Hold the cloth to light: if it passes through, air will pass through too, and with it, heat can escape. A tightly woven cotton oxford blocks light almost completely; a loosely woven tropical wool glows. The uncomfortable truth for cotton loyalists is that weave structure matters more than fiber mythology — and wool, given the right construction, might be the cooler choice in every sense of the word.</p>
