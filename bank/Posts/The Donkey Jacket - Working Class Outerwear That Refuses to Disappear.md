## Content

<p>The donkey jacket is a short, unlined wool jacket with a PVC or leather yoke across the shoulders and upper back. It was standard issue for outdoor laborers in Britain — construction workers, road crews, dock workers — from the mid-twentieth century onward. The wool body provided warmth. The reinforced yoke shed rain and resisted the abrasion of carrying heavy loads on the shoulders.</p>
<p>The jacket&#8217;s construction is deliberately crude. No lining, no internal structure, no style lines. The front closes with buttons — often mismatched replacements after the originals fell off. The pockets are large, unlined, and positioned for hands rather than aesthetics. Nothing about a donkey jacket suggests any consideration beyond function.</p>
<p>This absolute lack of pretension became, paradoxically, the jacket&#8217;s appeal beyond its working origins. Musicians, artists, and students adopted it precisely because it signaled indifference to fashion. The donkey jacket&#8217;s meaning came from what it wasn&#8217;t — it wasn&#8217;t designed, it wasn&#8217;t branded, and it wasn&#8217;t trying to be anything other than warm and durable.</p>
<p>Finding a genuine donkey jacket today requires effort. The original manufacturers have largely ceased production as demand from the building trades shifted to high-visibility safety wear. What remains is a garment that did its job without compromise for decades, and asks nothing of its wearer except that they put it on when it&#8217;s cold.</p>
