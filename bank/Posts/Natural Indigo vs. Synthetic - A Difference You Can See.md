## Content

<p>The chemical compound is identical. Both natural and synthetic indigo are indigotin — the same molecule, the same blue. Yet fabric dyed with natural indigo and fabric dyed with synthetic indigo look different, feel different, and age differently. The molecule is the same, but the process is not.</p>
<p>Natural indigo extraction produces a complex mixture. Alongside pure indigotin, the fermented leaf matter yields indirubin — a reddish compound — and other trace molecules that subtly shift the color toward warmth. Synthetic indigo is pure indigotin only, which reads cooler and flatter against cloth.</p>
<p>The dyeing method matters more than the dye source. Natural indigo vats ferment over days, creating a living chemical environment that deposits color unevenly at the molecular level. Each dip is slightly different. This micro-variation gives naturally dyed fabric a depth and complexity that uniform synthetic application cannot produce.</p>
<p>Whether this difference justifies the cost — natural indigo can be fifty times more expensive than synthetic — depends on what you&#8217;re making and who it&#8217;s for. For production denim at scale, synthetic indigo is the only practical choice. For a small-batch maker dyeing twenty meters of cloth, the natural process produces results that have no synthetic equivalent.</p>
