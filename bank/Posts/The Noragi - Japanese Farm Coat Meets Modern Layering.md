## Content

<p>The noragi was a working garment worn by Japanese farmers, constructed from whatever fabric was available — often patched together from multiple indigo-dyed cottons. Its wraparound front, tied with a simple sash, made it easy to put on and remove. The wide body allowed unrestricted movement for planting and harvesting.</p>
<p>What distinguishes the noragi from Western workwear is its relationship with imperfection. Patches weren&#8217;t hidden — they were displayed. Repairs used visible stitching, often in contrasting thread, following the boro tradition of making mends beautiful. A well-worn noragi told a story of labor and resourcefulness.</p>
<p>Contemporary designers have adopted the noragi silhouette for its effortless drape and versatility. The wraparound front works over a t-shirt or a button-down. The relaxed shoulders suit modern proportions. The kimono-influenced sleeves create a silhouette that feels neither Eastern nor Western but simply comfortable.</p>
<p>The best modern interpretations respect the original construction: natural fibers, minimal structure, and a fit that improves with washing and wear. A noragi shouldn&#8217;t look precious. It should look like something you&#8217;d actually work in.</p>
