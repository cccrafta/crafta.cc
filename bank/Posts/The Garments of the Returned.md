## Content

<p>The suits in Peaky Blinders are not what working-class Birmingham men wore in 1919. They are what those men aspired to, and that distinction is where the real garments begin. Stephanie Collie, the show's first costume designer, sourced tweed from Scottish mills, had Cillian Murphy's suits made bespoke by a Savile Row-trained tailor named Keith Watson, and hunted vintage fairs for period-appropriate horn buttons and cufflinks rather than accepting contemporary reproductions. She described her approach in a single word: heightening. Making visible what the original aspiration was. This is what the show does with its cloth: not invent, but clarify.</p>

<p>Small Heath, Birmingham, 1919. The war has been over for a year. The Birmingham Small Arms factory on Armoury Road, which spent four years making rifles, is still running. The men who operated those machines, or who carried them across France, are back, in the same terraced streets, wearing whatever they returned with. A working-class man of that period typically owned one suit, one overcoat, one pair of boots. The collar of his shirt was detachable, not for style, but because the collar was the only part that required frequent laundering, and laundering cost time and money. He wore it on Sundays and kept it from the week's work. And yet photographs and accounts from Small Heath in this period document men who dressed with deliberate care: silk neck scarves, carefully worn flat caps, tweed jackets maintained to the limit of their condition. The aspiration was genuine, and so was the constraint; both are present in the cloth.</p>

<p>Tweed is the fabric at the centre of this. It arrived in British cities from Scotland and Ireland, where it was hand-woven by farmers and fishermen as working cloth, heavy, felted, resistant to wind and wet. By the early twentieth century it had migrated from country to city through the tailoring trade: the shooting jacket reformed as the lounge jacket, and tweed became the fabric of a three-piece suit that a working man could afford and a gentleman could wear without embarrassment. In the show's wardrobe, the herringbone tweed suit is rendered in full: a two-button single-breasted jacket with notch lapels, a six-button waistcoat with adjustable rear strap, and high-waisted flat-front trousers with a short break. The cloth itself runs 12-16oz — heavy enough to hold the shape of loaded pockets, to resist a damp Birmingham winter, to soften over years of wear rather than deteriorate. Self-covered buttons on both jacket and waistcoat, a detail that keeps the surface of the cloth uninterrupted, refusing the contrast that a plain button would introduce. The palette runs to charcoal, chocolate brown, dove grey. Nothing shouts. The cloth does the work.</p>

<p>The overcoat tells a more complicated story. What Collie dressed her lead character in was a long, structured coat in heavy wool, worn open or fastened with a single button at the waist, with wide peak lapels and a half collar in black velvet. The velvet collar is a collar form historically reserved for finer overcoats, associated with the British upper classes and with the kind of tailoring that working men in Small Heath would never have commissioned. The show applies it to a man who grew up in poverty and runs a criminal gang. The contradiction is not an error; it is the show's central argument about these men: they took the materials of authority and wore them on their own terms. The overcoat's drape was equally deliberate. Collie cited Sergio Leone's Western aesthetic: garments tailored lighter than their weight so they moved dramatically. The result is a coat with the bearing of a military garment and the presence of a film character: constructed for authority, cut for the camera.</p>

<p>The club collar shirt and the newsboy cap complete the picture differently: through restraint rather than elevation. The club collar is a rounded, stiff white detachable collar, held with a gold stud at the throat. Its form is the form of post-war formality: men who had worn military uniforms for four years brought a residual discipline to civilian dress. The collar that needed to be laundered separately, that had to be attached deliberately, that fastened with a stud rather than a button — it imposes a ritual on dressing that cannot be performed without attention. The newsboy cap, by contrast, is the garment the Shelbys never give up. Its construction — eight fabric panels sewn to a central button, fuller in the crown than a flat cap, soft and wide — marks it as working-class headwear. As the Shelbys climb through the series from gang to business to Parliament, they can afford fedoras. They do not wear them. The cap is kept not from necessity but from choice, and that choice is a position: we came from here, and that is not something to be hidden or discarded.</p>

<p>The impact of all this on contemporary menswear is not incidental. Since the show premiered in 2013, flat cap sales surged repeatedly. Tweed suit demand increased. The club collar appeared in contemporary collections; Gucci and Ralph Lauren incorporated the heritage tailoring silhouette into their lines. Viberg and Division Road collaborated on a derby boot directly inspired by the show's aesthetic. But the more significant response came from the makers who looked at the garments and found the logic behind them. Bryceland's in Tokyo cuts safari jackets in Scottish Solaro wool and bespoke suits in English hopsack — the same cloth tradition, the same refusal to substitute. SEH Kelly makes working jackets from sixth-generation Donegal tweed, with horn buttons sourced from the last button-makers in Britain, relocated from Birmingham to the Cotswolds. The Armoury builds three-piece logic into everything they commission. These makers are not referencing the show. They are referencing what the show pointed toward: a moment in British textile and tailoring history when the design problems were real, the solutions were honest, and the cloth was chosen because nothing else would do.</p>

<p>The garments of 1919 Birmingham traveled because their argument is intact. Tweed justifies itself through what it does: holds shape under load, softens with wear, honest in every dimension of its construction. The three-piece suit carries its own authority without requiring a backdrop of wealth. The club collar imposes the discipline of dressing deliberately. The cap kept in preference to the fedora is the gesture that names everything: identity as origin, not destination. Peaky Blinders heightened these garments to make them visible. What became visible was already there, waiting in the cloth, in the mills that wove it, in the men who wore it back from the war and dressed with what they had and what they intended. For anyone making garments, this is the instruction the show points toward: the men of Small Heath chose tweed not because it was beautiful but because nothing else could handle the weight, the cold, and the work; and that is the only reason a material should ever be chosen.</p>
