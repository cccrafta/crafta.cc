## Content

<p>Most leather today is chrome-tanned — a process that takes hours or days and produces a uniform, soft material. Vegetable tanning takes weeks or months, using tree bark, leaves, and other organic matter to transform raw hides into leather. The difference isn&#8217;t just speed. It&#8217;s character.</p>
<p>Vegetable-tanned leather starts stiff and pale. Over time — through handling, sun exposure, and the oils from your skin — it develops a rich patina that chrome-tanned leather never achieves. A vegetable-tanned wallet at five years old looks nothing like it did new, and that transformation is the entire point.</p>
<p>The tanning process itself is ancient. Pits filled with tannin-rich solutions — oak bark, mimosa, chestnut — slowly penetrate the hide over weeks. The tanner adjusts concentrations gradually, moving hides through progressively stronger solutions. Rush the process and the leather becomes brittle. Do it right and the result is leather that lasts generations.</p>
<p>There&#8217;s a reason the best leather workshops in Tuscany, England, and Japan still use this method despite its inefficiency. Vegetable tanning produces a material that responds to its owner. It&#8217;s leather that has a future, not just a present.</p>
