## Content

<p>Linen is made from flax, a plant cultivated for fiber since at least 8000 BCE. Fragments of linen cloth have been found in prehistoric lake dwellings in Switzerland. Egyptian mummies were wrapped in it. Roman soldiers wore it under their armor. It is, by a significant margin, the oldest textile fiber in continuous human use.</p>
<p>The properties that made linen valuable to ancient civilizations are the same ones that make it relevant today. Flax fiber is hollow, which allows it to absorb moisture rapidly — up to 20% of its weight — and release it quickly through evaporation. This makes linen feel cool against skin even in extreme heat. No synthetic fiber matches this performance.</p>
<p>Linen&#8217;s reputation for wrinkling is deserved but misunderstood. The fiber has low elasticity, meaning it doesn&#8217;t spring back after being compressed. But this characteristic is also what gives linen its distinctive drape — the fabric falls in sharp, architectural folds rather than soft curves. The wrinkles are part of the material&#8217;s character, not a defect.</p>
<p>Good linen improves dramatically with washing. New linen feels crisp and slightly stiff. After ten washes it softens considerably. After fifty it becomes almost silky. A linen shirt at five years old is a different garment than it was new — better in every measurable way.</p>
