## Content

<p>Turn a Harrington inside out and the Fraser tartan is the first thing you notice. Red, green, blue — the weave is lighter than you expect, smooth against the back of the hand. The pattern dates to the 13th century; Lord Lovat approved its commercial use in 1938, and it has not changed since. The outside is plain (cotton blouson, stand collar, zip front, ribbed cuffs and waist) but it is the tartan lining that tells you what you're holding. The two-button stand collar — what Baracuta calls the "dog ear" — sits differently from a shirt collar or a bomber's ribbed neck; buttoned, it closes flat against the throat and seals out wind, unbuttoned it folds into soft points that frame the neck without flopping. The jacket wasn't called a Harrington until 1965, when John Simons wrote a felt-tip note in his Richmond shop window calling it the "Rodney Harrington" after Ryan O'Neal's character in the American soap opera Peyton Place. The name stuck; the original designation, Baracuta G9, did not.</p>

<p>John and Isaac Miller made rain coats for Burberry and Aquascutum on Beswick Street in Manchester before starting Baracuta in the mid-1930s. Manchester in the 1930s was still a textile city; the Millers knew cotton and construction, knew how a coat should sit on a body. When they joined Manchester Golf Club, they noticed the problem: full-length coats restricted the swing. Their answer was the G9 — G for Golf, 9 for nine holes — with a short body that sat above the waist, raglan sleeves that ran collar to cuff without a shoulder seam, and an umbrella back yoke. McGregor in America had a similar idea with the Drizzler in 1947, but used a point collar instead of a stand collar and skipped the tartan lining. The G9 outlasted it because the stand collar provided better wind protection and the tartan gave the jacket an interior identity that plain-lined competitors lacked. By the late 1940s the G9 had crossed the Atlantic; Bob Hope and Bing Crosby wore them on American golf courses before the jacket had any association with youth culture at all. In Japan it picked up a different name entirely — the "Swing Top" or "Swing Jacket," after the golf swing it was designed to accommodate.</p>

<p>The yoke is modeled after an actual umbrella. A cape-like panel spans the shoulders with a center vent; rain hits the panel and sheds sideways instead of soaking through at the seams. The fabric is plain cotton, not waxed or coated. Unlike a Barbour, which relies on wax impregnation for waterproofing, the G9 uses the angle and overlap of the cut itself. The raglan sleeves support the yoke's function: because there is no shoulder seam, the yoke's surface runs unbroken from side to side. Pick the jacket up by the collar and the yoke hangs like a cape, the vent visible at center back. The difference between this and a standard bomber like the MA-1 is structural: the bomber has a flat back panel, while the Harrington's yoke adds a second layer across the shoulders that directs water and allows air to move underneath.</p>

<p>Every other golf jacket of the period had plain fabric inside; the Fraser tartan was immediate differentiation. Tartan hides wear better than solid colors. And by securing permission from Simon Fraser, Lord Lovat and 24th Chieftain of Clan Fraser, they put a 13th-century clan pattern inside a Manchester golf jacket. When Lord Lovat later became a decorated WWII commander, that connection gained prestige the Millers could not have planned. The lining has not changed since 1938: same pattern, same colors, and still the way you identify the original.</p>

<p>The original G9 shell was high-thread-count cotton; polyester did not exist yet. Rub the fabric of a vintage G9 between your fingers and it feels dense, almost papery, with a dry hand that softens over years of wear. A G9 that has been worn for a decade shows it: the cotton relaxes at the elbows and across the back, the collar develops a permanent curve from being turned up, and the tartan fades first where the jacket folds flat in storage — along the center back and at the front placket. Modern G9s use 50/50 poly-cotton with a Coolmax tartan lining: faster drying and cheaper to make, though less breathable, and with a slight synthetic sheen the original never had. Alternatives still in production show what the 1937 cotton was like. Grenfell cloth from 1923 uses 600-thread Egyptian cotton gabardine, windproof but permeable to perspiration. Ventile, developed during WWII to keep downed RAF pilots alive in freezing water, achieves water resistance through fiber density alone: 100% cotton at 205g per square meter. Private White V.C. makes a Ventile Harrington in Manchester; holding it next to a current poly-cotton G9, the difference between a fabric that repels and one that breathes is immediate.</p>

<p>The short body that cleared a swing did not ride up on a Vespa; Mods in the early 1960s wore it over slim suits and the blouson cut held at speed. The raglan sleeves sat the same over a T-shirt, a polo, or a button-down. The elasticated cuffs and waist gave warmth without tailoring, fitting different builds without alteration. The stand collar went up for weather or down for casual. And the jacket was barely branded, which meant it did not telegraph price or allegiance and could cross subcultural lines without resistance. Skinheads in the mid-to-late 1960s wore it with Ben Sherman shirts and Dr. Martens in black, red, or tan; working-class uniform before the movement turned political. Punks in the 1970s inherited it from skinheads: same jacket, worn rough instead of pressed. The paper "Put on Your Boots and Harrington!" traces the material continuity between these movements, and the Harrington runs through it more than almost any other garment.</p>

<p>Hollywood put the jacket on four different men in thirteen years. James Dean wore a red McGregor Drizzler, the American point-collar competitor, in Rebel Without a Cause in 1955; coupled with jeans and a white T-shirt, it became shorthand for teenage defiance. Elvis wore a Baracuta G9 in King Creole in 1958 because the blouson shape moved on camera without pulling at the seams. Steve McQueen wore a navy G9 on the cover of Life in 1963 and in The Thomas Crown Affair in 1968: functional and plain, which was McQueen's whole approach to clothes. Frank Sinatra wore one in Assault on a Queen in 1966, easy and unforced, as though the jacket had always been in his wardrobe. Four men with nothing in common except the jacket, and it fit all of them without a single change to the design.</p>

<p>Baracuta still makes the G9 in England and parts of Europe, though some production has moved to Italy. The company went through several owners after the Miller era; WP Lavori now holds the brand and has maintained UK manufacturing for the core G9 line. Private White V.C. in Manchester uses Ventile cotton with RIRI zippers from Switzerland, the closest thing to what the original must have been, and made in the same city where the Millers started. Fred Perry cuts a slimmer silhouette with the laurel wreath on the chest, while Boncoura in Osaka (founded 2011) goes the other direction with vintage reproductions in custom over-dyed cotton/nylon, each jacket finished by hand. Tom Ford's version, worn by Daniel Craig in Quantum of Solace, uses black nylon with leather trim, based on the G4 (Baracuta's 1944 open-waist alternative) rather than the G9.</p>

<p>What copies get wrong is usually the back yoke. A flat panel does not shed water; only the umbrella geometry works. The collar height and button placement determine whether it stays up or falls, and the tartan needs specific alignment and weight because cheap versions just use generic check. The elasticated ribbing needs correct tension: too loose and the blouson shape goes slack, too tight and it binds. The short body has to hit just above the waist, which means the pattern accounts for torso length, not just chest. These tolerances exist because the Miller brothers were solving a specific problem in 1937 — keeping golfers dry and unrestricted — and that problem has precise answers. The umbrella yoke still sheds rain the same way and the raglan sleeves still give full rotation. The Fraser tartan is still the exact pattern Lord Lovat approved in 1938. Nothing in the G9 is there for decoration: the yoke sheds rain, the sleeves allow rotation, the tartan differentiates, the short body clears the swing. Coat-makers built it to handle rain on a golf course, and that practical specificity, rather than any design ambition, is what gave the jacket a life well beyond the course.</p>
