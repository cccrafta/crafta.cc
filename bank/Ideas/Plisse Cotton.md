## Angle
Plissé and seersucker both pucker, but through fundamentally different means. Seersucker is structural (slack and tight warp yarns during weaving). Plissé is chemical (caustic soda applied after weaving shrinks treated areas, untreated areas pucker upward). The distinction matters: seersucker's texture survives ironing; plissé's can flatten. A materials post exploring the chemistry, the ancient Egyptian lineage, Fortuny's revival, and Issey Miyake's Homme Plissé line.

## Key Facts
- Texture produced by applying sodium hydroxide (caustic soda) in controlled patterns — stripes, checks, dots
- Chemical causes treated areas to shrink, untreated areas pucker upward, then heat-set
- Traces to ancient Egyptian pleated textiles (c. 3000 BCE), though chemical process emerged in 19th-century France
- Mariano Fortuny revived pleated fabrics in early 20th century with Delphos gowns
- Puckered surface lifts off skin, promotes airflow, resists visible creasing
- Softer and more uniform crinkle than seersucker, but less durable under tension/laundering
- Issey Miyake's Homme Plissé is the most prominent contemporary use
