## Angle
The existing Crafta post covers seersucker's material science (how the pucker works, why it cools). This idea covers the cultural migration — a fabric named from Persian "shir o shekar" (milk and sugar), woven in Mughal India, adopted as working-class cloth in American colonies, flipped into preppy icon by 1920s college students, and codified as Southern identity through Haspel's New Orleans factory. The class inversion is the hook: at every stage, seersucker's meaning was redefined by who wore it.

## Key Facts
- Name derives from Persian "shir o shekar" (milk and sugar) — describes the alternating smooth and puckered texture
- Woven in Mughal India, reached American colonies in 18th century
- Initially working-class clothing in the US — cheap, no-iron, practical
- 1920s college students adopted it as reverse snobbery
- Haspel (New Orleans, c. 1909) turned it into the Southern gentleman's uniform
- Senate Seersucker Thursday tradition ran 1996-2012
- Japanese parallel: shijira-ori, a Meiji-era textile with similar airflow properties developed independently in Tokushima
- Okayama mills now produce wider-puckered premium seersucker for export
