## The Idea

Passementerie is the craft of decorative trimmings: tassels, braids, fringes, gimp, bullion. Not a minor craft — historically, it was as skilled and valued as tailoring. It adorned the interiors of palaces, the uniforms of armies, the ceremonial dress of courts.

The Huguenots brought it to London's Spitalfields and Bethnal Green in the late 17th and early 18th centuries when they fled Catholic persecution in France. They settled around Brick Lane, working in attics. The East End became one of Europe's centers for passementerie.

Now: one workshop. Jessica Light has been working in the trade for 30 years, trained under Wendy Cushing. She makes everything by hand, to commission.

## Why It's Interesting for Crafta

This is the extreme end of craft survival — not a dozen practitioners, not a regional tradition hanging on. One person. The specific geographic and cultural history (Huguenot refugee craft, East End textile traditions, Spitalfields heritage) is rich. The garments this trim appeared on are the context Crafta already covers: military uniforms, formal dress, ceremonial outerwear.

The other angle: passementerie teaches something about hierarchy of craft. Trim was an entire industry. The skill required to weave a tassel is comparable to the skill required to weave the cloth it decorates. We've forgotten that.

## Key Facts
- Technique origins: 16th century France
- Arrives in London: late 17th/early 18th century via Huguenot refugees
- Traditional location: Spitalfields and Bethnal Green, around Brick Lane
- Current practitioner: Jessica Light ("Tassel Queen of Bethnal Green")
- Training: under Wendy Cushing, 30 years in the trade
- Notable commissions: Buckingham Palace balcony drape fringe, Vivienne Westwood macramé belts, 'Death on the Nile' film fringed tablecloths
- Status: Heritage Crafts' endangered list (red)
- London Craft Week 2026: featured in "Living Legacies" endangered crafts programme
