## The Idea

Bogolanfini is technically a resist process, but with chemistry instead of blocking. Here's the sequence:

1. Handwoven cotton strips are joined into cloth
2. Cloth is soaked in a bath of mashed n'gallama tree leaves — turns yellow
3. Dried in the sun
4. Iron-rich Niger River mud (fermented in clay jars for up to a year, collected in March when spring flooding brings fresh silt from Guinea) is painted onto specific areas
5. The iron in the mud reacts chemically with the tannin in the plant dye — produces a stable dark brown-black
6. Mud is washed off — the dark pattern remains from the chemical reaction, not from the mud itself
7. Yellow areas around the pattern are then bleached with soap or bleach, lightening further
8. The final result: dark geometric patterns on a light or creamy ground

The pattern is produced by what you apply, but it persists because of chemistry, not pigment.

## Why the Counterintuitive Part Matters

In most dyeing, you apply color where you want color. In bogolanfini, you apply mud where you want dark — but the dark isn't the mud. It's what happens when iron meets tannin. The mud is the catalyst, not the colorant. And it washes off. The color that stays is a reaction between two things applied at different stages.

## Cultural Dimension

Traditional gender division: men weave the cloth on narrow looms (strips ~15cm wide, stitched together), women do all the dyeing. Worn by hunters for ritual protection and camouflage. Women wrapped in it after initiation and after childbirth — the cloth believed to absorb dangerous forces.

In the 1970s, six art students in Bamako formed Groupe Kasobane, explicitly rejecting the Eurocentric curriculum. They reframed mudcloth as a contemporary painting medium — expanded from geometric symbols to nature scenes and narrative imagery. Made it a vehicle for economic independence.

## Key Facts
- "Bògo" = mud/earth, "lan" = made with, "fini" = cloth (Bambara language)
- Tradition: 12th century, Bambara people of Mali
- Niger River mud fermented up to a year in clay jars
- Collected in March when spring flooding brings fresh silt from Guinea
- The chemical reaction: iron (mud) + tannin (plant dye) = stable dark color
- Pattern width of strips: ~15cm, stitched together to ~1m × 1.5m cloth
- Groupe Kasobane: 1970s Bamako art students who modernized the tradition
