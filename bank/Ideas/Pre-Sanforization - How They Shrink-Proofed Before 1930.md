## The Idea

Sanforization (named for Sanford Cluett, patented 1930) is now the standard for pre-shrinking woven cotton. The process uses a rubber belt and steam to mechanically compact the fabric before cutting — resulting in controlled, predictable shrinkage that can be specified to within 1%.

But the 1929 Sears Roebuck catalogue says "pre-shrunk, both by water and steam." This is one year before Sanforization. What were they doing?

Pre-Sanforization shrinking methods:
1. **London shrink** (or cold water shrink) — wetting the fabric, rolling it in damp cloth for 12-24 hours, then pressing. Common for woolens.
2. **Steam shrink** — steaming the fabric on a press or decatizing machine — relaxes the tension set during weaving
3. **Wet finishing** — washing the fabric in water at the mill to allow residual shrinkage

None of these were as reliable as Sanforization. A water-and-steam pre-shrunk garment in 1929 would still shrink 2-5% more in the first wash — which is why Sears also sold overalls "cut full and roomy... when you get them, they stay that way." The generous cut was compensating for the residual shrinkage the pre-shrinking didn't remove.

The interesting question: why did Sanforization spread so quickly after 1930? Because it solved the compensation problem — you could now cut to fit, not to compensate.
