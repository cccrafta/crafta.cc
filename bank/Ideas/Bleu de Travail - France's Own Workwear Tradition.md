## The Idea

Crafta has the chore coat post (French origin, American adoption). But the deeper French workwear story — the bleu de travail tradition — is different and untold.

**The tradition**: French workers from the 1880s through the mid-20th century wore indigo-dyed cotton twill or moleskin in blue as standard working dress. The color blue was practical (hid stains, held dye well with indigo) and became culturally associated with the working class.

**Key garments**:
- **The veste de travail** (work jacket) — single-breasted, minimal collar, practical pockets, usually moleskin or cotton drill
- **The tablier** (work apron) — cross-back construction, indigo blue
- **The salopette** (dungarees) — French equivalent of overalls, different cut
- **The Moleskine trouser** — Crafta has the Moleskin post; the trouser specific to French workwear deserves its own coverage

**Key brands with genuine lineage**:
- **Le Laboureur** (est. 1956, but tradition goes to late 19th century) — the most authentic
- **Adolphe Lafont** (est. 1844) — possibly the oldest surviving workwear brand in the world
- **Vetra** (est. 1927) — Paris, now made in Portugal
- **Bleu de Paname** — contemporary brand, genuine historical research behind designs

**The difference from American workwear**: The French tradition is more tailored, the cut closer, the materials softer. Moleskin (a brushed cotton with a suede-like face) is the defining French workwear fabric — American workwear is denim and canvas. The French garments are also more directly descended from working-class dress without the romantic reinterpretation American workwear went through.
