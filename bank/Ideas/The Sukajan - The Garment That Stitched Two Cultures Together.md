## The Idea

The sukajan (スカジャン, from "Yokosuka" + "jumper") originated in Yokosuka — home of the largest US naval base in Japan — in the late 1940s. American GIs brought their military-issue flight jackets and MA-1 blousons to local embroiderers and had them decorated with Japanese motifs: dragons, tigers, carp, cherry blossoms, Mount Fuji, geishas, maps of Japan and Korea.

The craft context: Japanese embroidery traditions (particularly Nishijin weaving and Kyoto silk work) were among the finest in the world. The embroiderers were applying centuries-old technique to foreign garment forms for foreign patrons. The imagery the GIs chose was often Western fantasy-Japan — dragons and samurai imagery that Japanese people didn't wear themselves.

Over time, Japanese people adopted the garment. By the 1960s-70s it was part of Japanese youth culture — specifically the *bosozoku* (biker gangs) and *yankii* subcultures. The garment had migrated: made for Americans by Japanese, then reclaimed by Japanese youth as their own.

**The Crafta angle**: This is a garment post about material and cultural exchange. The construction (silk-backed satin shell, hand embroidery, rayon lining) is specific. The origin in Yokosuka is specific. The migration from occupation souvenir to Japanese street culture is the cultural arc.
