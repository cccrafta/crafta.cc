## The Idea

Most fabric color comes from dye — chemical compounds that absorb certain wavelengths and reflect others. Structural color is different: it comes from the physical geometry of a surface interacting with light. Butterfly wings, peacock feathers, beetle shells — all use micro-scale structure to produce color without pigment.

Trilobal yarn is a synthetic version of this principle. The cross-section of the filament is triangular (three lobes) rather than circular. Each face reflects light at a different angle. As the fabric moves, different faces catch the light — producing the iridescent, color-shifting effect of Stone Island's Nylon Metal.

Stone Island has used this since 2005. Their "Tinto Capo" process dyes the finished garment rather than the yarn — garment-level dyeing means the color settles into the structure of the finished piece, which is why it shifts as you move.

## Why This Matters for Crafta

This is a Materials post that bridges heritage craft and technical innovation. The principle of structural color — geometry doing what pigment can't — is ancient (silk's natural sheen comes partly from its triangular filament cross-section). Stone Island engineered this deliberately. The post can open with silk's natural trilobal structure and work forward to synthetic application.

## Key Facts
- Standard nylon: circular cross-section, reflects light uniformly
- Trilobal nylon: triangular cross-section, three faces reflect light at different angles → iridescence
- Stone Island Nylon Metal debut: 2005
- "Tinto Capo" process: garment-level dyeing (not yarn dyeing)
- 2026: updated with ECONYL® recycled regenerated nylon — same trilobal structure, recycled input
- Natural analogue: silk has a triangular cross-section (that's where its natural sheen comes from)
- Other examples of structural color: butterfly wings, peacock feathers, beetle shells
