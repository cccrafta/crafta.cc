## The Idea

Denim's origin story is wrong in popular telling. The myth: "denim" comes from "serge de Nîmes" (Nîmes, France). The reality is more complicated:

- **The fabric**: The sturdy twill used for jeans likely came from Genoa, Italy — "jeans" derives from "Genes" (the old English name for Genoa). Nîmes made a similar but different fabric.
- **The American invention**: Jacob W. Davis, a Latvian-born tailor in Reno, Nevada, invented the riveted trouser in 1871. A customer's husband kept tearing the pockets of his work pants. Davis used copper rivets from a horse blanket to reinforce the stress points.
- **The partnership**: Davis couldn't afford the $68 patent fee. He wrote to Levi Strauss & Co. in San Francisco — his fabric supplier — and proposed splitting the patent. Strauss agreed. Patent granted May 20, 1873.
- **The Rivet War**: The patent expired in 1890. Immediately, every competitor started making riveted pants. Lee, Carhartt, and dozens of others entered the market. Levi's spent 17 years in litigation trying to extend their monopoly.
- **The Big Three**: By the 1920s, Levi's (West), Lee (Midwest), and Wrangler (South/West) had divided the market geographically.

**The 1929 Sears connection**: By 1929, even Sears' private-label "Hercules" overalls were sold in competition with all three. The Carhartt patent (1915) shows how systematically the construction details were being engineered and protected simultaneously.
