## The Idea

The guayabera has two identities and three countries of origin — and none of them agree. It's worn untucked over trousers (fieldwork practical), but in Cuba it IS formalwear (you'd wear it to a business meeting instead of a suit). This is a garment that crossed the line between workwear and formal dress completely, without changing form.

The design is specific: two vertical columns of alforzas (tiny pintucks — 10 pleats each, sewn closely together) running the full length of front and back. Four patch pockets. Worn untucked. The alforzas require skilled, precise hand stitching. They're the defining construction detail.

The pockets, per the most persistent origin story: a farmer's wife added them to her husband's guayabera so he could carry guavas from the field. Agricultural utility, now encoded into formal dress.

## The Contested Origin

Three claims:
1. **Cuba** (contested) — documentary evidence from 1880 and 1893; Cuba made it "official formal dress" in 2010
2. **Yucatán, Mexico** (most cited) — Mérida is often named as origin city
3. **Philippines** (plausible) — via the Manila-Acapulco galleon trade, the barong tagalog shares structural similarities; Filipino design may have arrived in Mexico via the trade route, then spread to Cuba and Caribbean

No single claim is conclusive.

## Crafta Angle

The guayabera teaches something about how garments migrate across categories. The pockets were for guavas. The alforzas were ornament added to elevate a working shirt. Somewhere between Cuba's fields and its presidential palaces, the shirt became both things at once. A Garments post that uses this identity tension as its spine.

## Key Facts
- Two vertical columns of alforzas (10 pleats each) front and back — defining construction detail
- Four patch pockets
- Worn untucked over trousers
- Linen or lightweight cotton — tropical climate garment
- Cuba: reinstated as "official formal dress garment" in 2010
- Dominican Republic, Mexico, Puerto Rico all treat it as part of traditional dress
- Contested origin: Cuba, Yucatán (Mexico), Philippines (via Manila-Acapulco galleon trade)
