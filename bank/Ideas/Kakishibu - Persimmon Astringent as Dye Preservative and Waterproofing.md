## The Idea

Kakishibu is pressed juice from unripe persimmons, fermented for months or years. The tannin content is extremely high — it reacts with iron mordant to produce deep brown tones, and with exposure to UV light continues to darken over time. A garment dyed with kakishibu gets darker the more sun it sees. The color develops with wear rather than fading.

But kakishibu isn't only a dye. Historically:
- **Fabric**: waterproofing treatment for cotton and paper
- **Fishing nets**: preservation against salt water degradation
- **Wood**: antibacterial and waterproofing sealant (used in traditional Japanese architecture)
- **Paper**: strengthens washi, used for umbrellas and lanterns
- **Food contact**: gourds and wooden vessels coated to preserve contents

This is a craft material with a unified logic — high tannin content solves multiple preservation problems — but it's rarely discussed outside Japanese craft circles.

## The Garment Angle

OLD FOLK HOUSE × BIOTOP (May 2026) layered kakishibu with iron mordant for "deep coloring with depth and dimension." Hand-application means each piece differs — the iron mordant reaction varies with temperature and timing. Not a repeatable process in the industrial sense.

Also worth noting: kakishibu fabrics can't be machine washed with detergent — detergent disrupts the tannin bond. Care becomes part of the material's logic.
