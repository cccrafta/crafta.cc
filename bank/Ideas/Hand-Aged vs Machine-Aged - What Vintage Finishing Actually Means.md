## The Idea

"Vintage finish" is the most abused term in fashion. It can mean:
1. A machine-tumbled garment with pumice stones for 20 minutes
2. A laser-drawn fake fade pattern
3. A hand-applied process done by an artisan who understands how denim actually breaks down

The third is what Liberaiders is claiming. The question for a Crafta post: how do you actually tell the difference?

**Machine aging:**
- Stone wash: pumice stones in a tumble dryer create overall softening, but marks are random and distributed evenly (not how real wear works)
- Enzyme wash: biological agents eat cellulose, softening without stones — more even, less texture
- Sand blast: compressed sand on specific areas — achieves localized fading but the edges are too clean
- Laser distressing: precise patterns on denim — instantly obvious up close, too geometric
- Mechanical scraping: abrasion at stress points — close to real wear but marks follow a template

**Hand aging:**
- Artisan handles each piece individually, applying abrasion to where a specific person wearing a specific size would naturally show wear
- The marks follow anatomical logic (knee crease, seat, coin pocket rub, hem drag) rather than a pattern
- Results vary: no two pieces identical
- Up close: the fiber breakdown looks organic — fraying follows the weave direction, color loss is gradual not sudden

**The tell:** look at the back of the stressed area. Machine distressing often only affects the surface. Hand aging and real wear break the fiber from the inside out — the back shows color change too.

## The Broader Angle

This is also a post about what we mean when we say we want "character" in a garment. Character from wear is biographical — it records specific use. Fake character is theater. The post can ask: is there value in buying pre-aged garments at all, or does the biography need to be yours?
