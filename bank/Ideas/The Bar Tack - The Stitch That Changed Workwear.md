## The Idea

The bar tack is so common in workwear it's invisible. But it's worth asking: what exactly is it, why there, and why did it spread to every quality garment made since the 1890s?

A bar tack is a short, dense rectangular stitch — typically 30-40 threads in a 5-8mm × 2-3mm rectangle, created by a specialized sewing machine that stitches back and forth in a tight zigzag. It's placed at:
- Top corners of all patch pockets (where the pocket pulls away from the fabric under load)
- Belt loop ends
- Zipper and fly tops
- Seam ends at stress points

The 1929 Sears Roebuck catalogue mentions "bar tacked at all strain points" as a selling point across every grade of overall — from the cheapest children's at 53¢ to the premium Hercules. This means by 1929 it was standard enough to be marketable but not yet universal enough to be assumed.

The bar tack machine itself was an industrial development — it requires a specific mechanism to stitch back and forth densely. When did it become available to garment manufacturers and how quickly did it spread?
