## The Idea

The Donegal fleck — that speckled, multicolored effect in the yarn — is what makes Donegal tweed instantly recognizable. It's now a deliberate aesthetic choice and a protected tradition. But it started as poverty: weavers mixing whatever wool scraps they had, unable to afford waste. The look was born from economy, not design intention.

This is the Crafta version of "the constraint that became the aesthetic." Like the Harrington's umbrella yoke being engineering that became identity, the Donegal fleck is accident that became tradition.

The hand-weaving story is also interesting: when mechanical looms came to Ireland in the 1960s, hand-weaving went into rapid decline. Studio Donegal was founded as "an idealistic project to preserve the tradition." A hand-weaving course started in Kilcar was the first in Ireland since 1980. One course. One town. The chain of knowledge nearly broke.

## Key Facts
- Distinctive fleck: historically from blending leftover mixed-color wool scraps (economic necessity)
- Base material: wool from native Donegal sheep, traditionally dyed with moss, lichens, berries
- Weave technique: horizontal loom with flying shuttle (revolutionary when introduced, 18th century)
- 100% of authentic Donegal handwoven tweed still woven on the flying shuttle loom type
- 1880: Mrs. Ernest Hart establishes Donegal Industries Fund
- 1893: Improvement scheme in Ardara — elevated Donegal to international markets
- 1960s: Machine weaving arrives, hand-weaving declines rapidly
- Studio Donegal: founded to preserve hand-weaving against mechanization
- First hand-weaving course in Ireland since 1980: started in Kilcar
- The Permanent Style/Kilcar connection: Simon Crompton documented the course revival
