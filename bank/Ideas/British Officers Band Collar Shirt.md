## Angle
The band collar shirt sits at an intersection of British military utility, Irish working-class pragmatism, and Nehru's diplomatic signature. The design's power is subtraction — removing the collar rather than adding one. Trace the line from the WWI greyback flannel through 1920s industrial Britain to Oliver Spencer's contemporary grandad collar.

## Key Facts
- The "greyback" — a collarless flannel shirt — was standard British Army issue from WWI into WWII
- Officers purchased their own versions from 1914, worn under service dress tunics with ties
- 1908 service dress reforms exposed the shirt beneath the tunic for the first time
- 1920s-30s British and Irish working classes adopted the style — removable collars made laundering easier, stiff collars were dangerous in industrial settings
- Nehru's preference for muslin grandad collars in the 1950s brought the style into mainstream fashion
- Multiple names trace different cultural lines: band collar (military), grandad collar (working class), officer collar (diplomatic), mandarin collar (East Asian)
